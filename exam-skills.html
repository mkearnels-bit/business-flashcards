The Method

The PBLI Framework

Point, Because, Leads to, Impact: the structure that earns marks

How PBLI Maps to Skills

Each part of PBLI demonstrates a specific skill to the examiner

P POINT

📚 Knowledge (AO1)

State your business concept, theory, or key point. This is your foundation.

B BECAUSE

🎯 Application (AO2)

Link to the case study. Use the business name, their situation, and data from the extract.

L LEADS TO

🔍 Analysis (AO3)

Show the consequence. What happens next? Build the chain of cause and effect.

I IMPACT

🔍 Analysis (AO3)

The final effect on the business. Often relates to profit, sales, costs, or competitiveness.

💡 Example PBLI in action:

[P] Offering employee training can improve motivation [B] because TechStart's developers would feel more valued and gain new skills relevant to their app development work. [L] This leads to higher productivity as skilled, motivated employees work more efficiently, [I] which would help TechStart complete more projects and increase revenue, addressing their cash flow problems.

📌 Basic Application

Vaguely mentions the business or what they do without real detail

P: "Training improves motivation"
B: "because Nike's employees would feel valued"
L: "This leads to higher productivity"
I: "which increases profits"

❌ Only mentions the company name once. No real context used.

⭐ Advanced Application (Detailed Context)

Weaves specific details, data, and situation throughout the whole PBLI

P: "Training improves motivation particularly for a sportswear company like Nike where product knowledge is essential"
B: "because Nike's retail staff need to understand the technology in their running shoes to advise customers effectively"
L: "This leads to better customer service in Nike's flagship stores where customers expect expert advice"
I: "which increases sales and justifies Nike's premium pricing strategy"

✓ Context woven throughout. Every part links to the specific business.

Know Your Structure

What Each Mark Range Requires

Different marks need different structures. Know exactly what to write

6 Marks

Analyse / Explain

  • 2 × PBLI paragraphs
  • 3 marks per developed point
  • Focus on strong analysis chains
❌ No evaluation or conclusion needed
8 Marks

Discuss

  • 2 × PBLI paragraphs
  • Each PBLI needs balance (a "However")
  • The However can be a PBL or PBLI
❌ No conclusion needed at the end
10, 12, 20 Marks

Evaluate / Assess / Recommend

  • 2+ PBLI paragraphs each with balance
  • However = PBL for 10, PBLI for 12/20
  • More detailed context for 20 markers
✓ Justified conclusion is ESSENTIAL

The Finishing Touch

Writing Killer Conclusions

The conclusion is the last thing examiners read before checking the mark scheme. Make it count!

1

Make Your Decision

Start with a clear recommendation. Don't sit on the fence!

2

Justify with MOPS

Use a NEW factor from the case that outweighs your earlier points

3

Add Nuance

Use phrases like "In the short term... In the long term..." or "It depends on..."

🎯 MOPS Framework: Find Your Significant Factor

When writing your conclusion, find something from the case study about:

M

Market
Dynamic? Competitive? Growing?

O

Objectives
What does the business want?

P

Product
Type of product/service?

S

Situation
Current circumstances?

💡 Example Conclusion:

"Overall, I recommend TechStart accepts the venture capital investment. Given that TechStart develops mobile apps for small businesses in a technology sector where product lifecycles are short and customer expectations change rapidly, they are operating in an extremely dynamic market where well funded competitors like established software houses could easily replicate their app features within months. This means that the speed of growth enabled by £500,000 of VC funding significantly outweighs the ownership concerns discussed earlier, as without rapid scaling TechStart's current 540% revenue growth could quickly stall if competitors capture their target market of small businesses first. In the short term, the founders will sacrifice some equity and board control, but in the long term this investment could be the difference between TechStart becoming a market leader in small business apps or being overtaken by better resourced rivals. It depends on whether the two graduate founders can negotiate terms that protect their operational independence and product vision while securing the growth capital their tight cash flow situation desperately requires."

🔑 Key Phrases for Conclusions

In the short term... In the long term... It depends on... Given that... The key factor is... This outweighs... Overall, I recommend... On balance...

Exam Style Questions with Sample Answers

TechStart Ltd

TechStart Ltd is a software startup founded in 2022 by two university graduates. The company has grown from 3 to 15 employees in just two years. They develop mobile apps for small businesses. Revenue has increased from £50,000 to £320,000, but cash flow remains tight due to high development costs. The founders are now considering whether to seek £500,000 venture capital investment to accelerate growth, or continue growing organically through retained profits. The tech industry is known for being highly competitive and fast-moving.

Analyse the impact of rapid growth on TechStart's cash flow. (6 marks)

6 marks 2 × PBLI
❌ Weak Answer

TechStart has grown quickly which affects their cash flow. They have more employees now which costs more money. Their revenue has gone up a lot. Cash flow is important for businesses.

≈ 2/6 marks. No PBLI structure, no analysis chains

🎯
Reveal Skills in Answer
Tap to highlight each skill
✓ Model Answer (2 × PBLI)

PBLI 1: [P] Rapid growth increases working capital requirements [B] because TechStart has expanded from 3 to 15 employees, meaning salary costs have increased five-fold while they wait for client payments. [L] This leads to a timing gap where cash flows out for wages before revenue flows in from completed projects, [I] creating the "tight cash flow" mentioned despite their 540% revenue growth.

PBLI 2: [P] Growth often requires upfront investment in resources [B] because TechStart needs development tools, software licenses, and equipment for their expanding team. [L] This leads to large cash outflows before projects generate income, [I] which could force TechStart to delay hiring or turn down projects, limiting their ability to capitalise on market demand.

✓ 6/6 marks

Structure: 2 × PBLI = 6 marks (3 each)
No evaluation needed. Just strong analysis chains applied to context

Discuss whether TechStart should prioritise revenue growth or profitability. (8 marks)

8 marks 2 × PBLI + However
🎯
Reveal Skills in Answer
Tap to highlight each skill
✓ Model Answer (2 × PBLI with balance)

PBLI 1 + Balance: [P] Prioritising revenue growth can help a business establish market position [B] because TechStart operates in the fast-moving tech industry where their mobile apps for small businesses face constant competition from new entrants. [L] This leads to greater brand recognition among their target market of small businesses, [I] making it harder for competitors to take market share once TechStart is established as the go-to provider. However, [P] revenue without profitability creates cash flow risk. [B] TechStart already has "tight cash flow" despite their impressive 540% revenue growth. [L] This means chasing more revenue could actually worsen their cash position if they take on projects requiring upfront development costs before payment arrives.

PBLI 2 + Balance: [P] Prioritising profitability ensures business sustainability [B] because TechStart's founders started with limited resources as recent graduates and have no financial safety net if the business fails. [L] This leads to more stable cash reserves and reduced reliance on external funding, [I] giving the founders freedom to develop their app products without investor pressure to rush releases. However, [P] slow growth risks market irrelevance. [B] The case describes the tech industry as "highly competitive" where product lifecycles are short. [L] This means growing cautiously could allow well funded rivals to develop similar small business apps and capture customers before TechStart can scale.

✓ 8/8 marks

Structure: 2 × (PBLI + However + Balance) = 8 marks
Both sides shown throughout. NO conclusion needed for 8 mark Discuss

Evaluate whether TechStart should seek venture capital investment. (12 marks)

12 marks 2 × PBLI + Balance + Conclusion
❌ Weak Answer

TechStart should get venture capital because they need money. VC gives them £500,000. However, they might lose control. In conclusion, it depends.

≈ 3/12 marks. No PBLI structure, weak conclusion

🎯
Reveal Skills in Answer
Tap to highlight each skill
✓ Model Answer

PBLI 1 + Balance: [P] Venture capital provides significant funding for growth [B] because the £500,000 would solve TechStart's immediate cash flow problems and allow them to hire more of the specialist developers needed for their mobile app projects. [L] This leads to faster product development and ability to take on larger clients, [I] potentially accelerating their 540% growth trajectory even further and establishing market dominance in the small business app sector. However, [P] VCs typically want quick returns and board influence. [B] This could push TechStart towards aggressive growth that damages the collaborative culture they've built with their 15 employees. [L] This leads to potential conflict between founder vision and investor expectations, which could cause talented developers to leave if they feel the company culture has changed.

PBLI 2 + Balance: [P] Organic growth through retained profits maintains full ownership [B] because the two university graduate founders would keep complete control of their business direction without needing to report to external investors. [L] This leads to freedom to make long-term decisions without quarterly pressure, [I] protecting the original product vision that has already achieved impressive 540% revenue growth. However, [P] cash flow constraints limit growth capacity. [B] TechStart's "tight cash flow" means they may have to turn down lucrative projects or delay hiring when opportunities arise. [L] This leads to slower expansion, allowing better funded competitors to capture market share first in what the case describes as a "highly competitive" tech sector.

Conclusion (MOPS: Market): Overall, I recommend TechStart accepts the venture capital investment. Given that they operate in a dynamic, fast-moving technology market, speed is essential as competitors with better funding could develop similar products and capture market share before TechStart can respond. This market factor outweighs the ownership concerns. In the short term, they will sacrifice some control, but in the long term this investment could secure their market position. It depends on negotiating terms that let the founders retain operational control while accessing growth capital.

✓ 12/12 marks

Structure: 2 × (PBLI + Balance) + Justified Conclusion with MOPS
Conclusion uses Market factor + "In the short/long term" + "It depends on"

Green Bean Coffee

Green Bean Coffee is an independent coffee shop in Manchester that has built a loyal customer base over 5 years. Monthly revenue is £18,000 with profit margins of 15%. The owner, Sarah, is considering two options: opening a second location in a nearby town (requiring £80,000 investment) or franchising the Green Bean brand (with lower upfront costs but shared profits). Sarah currently works 60+ hours per week managing the business. Her main objective is to build a sustainable business that provides good work-life balance.

Analyse the benefits of franchising for Green Bean Coffee. (6 marks)

6 marks 2 × PBLI
🎯
Reveal Skills in Answer
Tap to highlight each skill
✓ Model Answer

PBLI 1: [P] Franchising allows expansion with lower capital requirements [B] because Sarah would avoid the £80,000 investment needed for a company-owned second location. [L] This leads to reduced financial risk as franchisees provide their own capital, [I] protecting Sarah's personal finances and the existing profitable business from potential losses if expansion fails.

PBLI 2: [P] Franchising reduces the operational burden on the business owner [B] because Sarah currently works 60+ hours per week, which is unsustainable. [L] This leads to franchisees handling day-to-day operations of new locations, [I] allowing Sarah to focus on brand development and quality control while potentially improving her work-life balance.

✓ 6/6 marks

Recommend whether Sarah should open a second location or franchise Green Bean. Justify your answer. (12 marks)

12 marks 2 × PBLI + Balance + Conclusion
🎯
Reveal Skills in Answer
Tap to highlight each skill
✓ Model Answer

PBLI 1 + Balance: [P] Opening a company-owned second location gives full control and profits [B] because Sarah would retain 100% of the 15% profit margin rather than sharing a proportion with franchisees for their management services. [L] This leads to higher potential earnings per location as all £2,700 monthly profit (15% of £18,000) goes to Sarah, [I] which could significantly increase her income and build a more valuable asset for eventual sale. However, [P] expansion requires significant capital investment. [B] The £80,000 required represents nearly 2.5 years of current annual profits (£32,400). [L] This creates substantial financial risk as Sarah would need to borrow or use all her savings, and if the new location fails she could lose everything including the successful original shop.

PBLI 2 + Balance: [P] Franchising offers expansion with reduced personal risk [B] because franchisees provide their own capital to open new Green Bean locations and take responsibility for daily operations. [L] This leads to potential for multiple locations generating royalty income without Sarah working additional hours, [I] directly addressing her current 60+ hour weeks that risk burnout and are clearly unsustainable long term. However, [P] franchisees may prioritise profit over quality. [B] Green Bean's loyal customer base in Manchester was built over 5 years through Sarah's personal attention to coffee quality and service. [L] If a franchisee cuts corners to boost their margins, poor reviews could spread online and damage the entire Green Bean brand reputation, hurting even Sarah's original shop.

Conclusion (MOPS:Objectives): Overall, I recommend Sarah pursues franchising. Given that her main objective is "sustainable business that provides good work-life balance", franchising directly addresses this goal while a second location would likely increase her already excessive workload. Her objective outweighs the potential for higher profits from direct ownership. In the short term, she will earn less per location, but in the long term she could have multiple franchises generating passive income while enjoying better quality of life. It depends on developing strong franchise agreements that protect brand quality.

✓ 12/12 marks

Structure: 2 × (PBLI + Balance) + Justified Conclusion with MOPS
Conclusion uses Objectives factor + "In the short/long term" + "It depends on"

SportsFit UK

SportsFit UK operates 12 mid-range gyms across Northern England with 15,000 members paying an average of £35/month. Recently, budget gym chains offering £15/month memberships have opened nearby, and fitness apps provide home workouts for £10/month. SportsFit has seen a 12% decline in membership over the past year. They are considering whether to lower prices, invest in premium facilities, or develop their own fitness app. SportsFit's core product is the personal, community-focused gym experience that budget gyms cannot replicate.

Discuss whether SportsFit should lower its prices to compete with budget gyms. (8 marks)

8 marks 2 × PBLI + However
🎯
Reveal Skills in Answer
Tap to highlight each skill
✓ Model Answer (No conclusion needed)

PBLI 1 + Balance: [P] Lowering prices could help retain price-sensitive customers [B] because SportsFit has lost 12% of their 15,000 members, with many likely switching to the new £15/month budget alternatives nearby. [L] This leads to maintaining membership numbers and spreading fixed costs like rent and equipment across more members, [I] which could stabilise revenue and prevent the spiral of declining memberships leading to gym closures. However, [P] dramatic price cuts destroy profit margins. [B] Matching £15 prices would mean losing £20 per member monthly, totalling £300,000 less revenue per month across their 15,000 members. [L] This would likely make their 12 mid-range gyms unprofitable as they have higher staffing, equipment maintenance, and facility costs than stripped-back budget competitors.

PBLI 2 + Balance: [P] Price competition can trigger destructive price wars [B] because budget gyms like PureGym are specifically designed for low-cost, high-volume operations while SportsFit has "mid-range" facilities with trained staff and quality equipment. [L] This leads to a race to the bottom where SportsFit is always at a cost disadvantage, [I] potentially forcing them out of business entirely as they simply cannot match competitors' cost efficiency without gutting the service that defines them. However, [P] targeted price adjustments differ from all-out price wars. [B] A moderate reduction to perhaps £30 might retain members who are tempted by budget options but still value SportsFit's community atmosphere. [L] This could stop the membership decline while preserving enough margin to maintain the mid-range positioning that differentiates them.

✓ 8/8 marks

Structure: 2 × (PBLI + However + Balance) = 8 marks
Discuss = show both sides. NO conclusion needed

Evaluate which strategy SportsFit should pursue to respond to increased competition. (12 marks)

12 marks 2 × PBLI + Balance + Conclusion
🎯
Reveal Skills in Answer

✓ Model Answer

PBLI 1 + Balance: [P] Developing a fitness app could create a new revenue stream [B] because apps like Peloton are attracting customers with £10/month home workout subscriptions that gym members can use anywhere without travelling to one of SportsFit's 12 locations. [L] This leads to potential to compete in the digital fitness market while giving existing members added value through hybrid membership, [I] diversifying income sources and making SportsFit more resilient to future shifts in how people exercise. However, [P] app development requires specialist capabilities. [B] SportsFit is a gym operator across Northern England with expertise in facilities and fitness coaching, not software development or digital marketing. [L] This means they would need to hire expensive developers or outsource, facing established apps with bigger budgets, recognisable brands, and years of content already built.

PBLI 2 + Balance: [P] Investing in premium facilities creates differentiation from budget competitors [B] because SportsFit could add personal trainers, spa facilities, and cutting-edge equipment that stripped-down £15 gyms simply cannot offer within their low-cost model. [L] This leads to a clear value proposition that justifies the £35 price point to quality-focused members, [I] potentially improving retention rates and even attracting new members who want more than a basic gym experience. However, [P] premium investment carries financial risk. [B] The 12% of members who already left were likely the most price-sensitive segment, and the 15,000 remaining may also be reconsidering their membership value. [L] This means SportsFit could be investing significant capital to serve what might be a shrinking market if the trend toward budget fitness continues across Northern England.

Conclusion (MOPS:Product): Overall, I recommend SportsFit invests in premium facilities. Given that their core product is "the personal, community-focused gym experience", this unique selling point cannot be replicated by budget gyms or apps. Investing in this strength plays to what makes SportsFit different, rather than competing in markets where they have no advantage. In the short term, investment costs will reduce profits, but in the long term a clear premium position creates sustainable competitive advantage. It depends on effectively marketing this premium positioning to attract members who value experience over price.

✓ 12/12 marks
