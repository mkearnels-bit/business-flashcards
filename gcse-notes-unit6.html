External Influences
6.1
The Economic Environment
How economic factors affect business

The Economic Environment

The economy affects businesses in many ways. When the economy is strong, people spend more money and businesses do well. When the economy is weak, people spend less and businesses struggle. Understanding the economic environment helps you predict how businesses will perform.

Interest Rates

The Bank of England sets the base rate of interest. Higher interest rates mean borrowing money becomes more expensive. Businesses are less likely to take out loans for expansion. Consumers also save more because savings accounts earn better returns. This reduces spending and hurts businesses.

Lower interest rates make borrowing cheaper. Businesses invest in new equipment and expansion. Consumers spend more instead of saving. This boosts business growth and employment.

Inflation

Inflation is when prices across the economy keep rising. It happens when there is too much money chasing too few goods. If inflation is high, raw materials cost more, so production becomes expensive. Businesses might pass these costs to customers through higher prices. This can reduce demand.

Moderate inflation (around 2%) is actually healthy for the economy. High inflation (above 5%) is harmful because it reduces people's ability to afford things and makes planning difficult.

Unemployment

When unemployment is high, fewer people have jobs and money to spend. This reduces demand for goods and services. Businesses might reduce staff or postpone expansion plans. High unemployment also means there is a larger pool of workers available, so businesses can find staff more easily.

When unemployment is low, people have money to spend and demand increases. However, finding skilled workers becomes harder and wages rise.

Economic Growth and GDP

Gross Domestic Product (GDP) measures the value of all goods and services produced in a country. When GDP grows, the economy is expanding. Businesses invest more, create more jobs, and earn higher profits. When GDP shrinks, the economy is contracting (recession).

The Business Cycle

The business cycle shows how economic activity changes over time. It has four main phases: boom, recession, slump, and recovery.

Boom Phase
  • GDP rising rapidly
  • Low unemployment
  • High consumer spending
  • Business investment up
  • Wages rising
Recession Phase
  • GDP starts falling
  • Unemployment rising
  • Consumer confidence drops
  • Business profits fall
  • Investment postponed
Slump Phase
  • GDP at lowest point
  • High unemployment
  • Very low spending
  • Business failures
  • Interest rates may fall
Recovery Phase
  • GDP starts rising
  • Unemployment falling
  • Spending picks up
  • Business confidence grows
  • Investment begins again
KEY TERM
Interest Rate

The cost of borrowing money, set by the Bank of England

KEY TERM
Inflation

Sustained increase in prices of goods and services over time

KEY TERM
Business Cycle

The recurring pattern of boom, recession, slump, and recovery

EXAM TIP

Learn the business cycle phases and what happens at each stage. You will likely get a question about how a specific phase affects a particular business. For example: How does a slump affect a luxury car manufacturer? Answer: High unemployment and low consumer confidence reduce demand for luxury goods, hurting sales.

REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE
The 2020 Pandemic

During the 2020 pandemic, the economy went into a sharp recession. Unemployment spiked, interest rates fell to near zero, and consumer spending dropped. Businesses like travel companies and restaurants suffered terribly. However, some businesses like online grocery and video streaming services thrived during this time.

Key Takeaways

  • Economic factors include interest rates, inflation, unemployment, and GDP growth
  • The business cycle shows how economies alternate between expansion and contraction
  • Different business types are affected differently by each phase of the cycle
  • Businesses must plan ahead to survive economic downturns
6.2
Legislation
Laws that protect employees, consumers, and competition

Why Does Legislation Matter?

Laws exist to protect employees, consumers, and competition. Businesses must follow these laws or face penalties, fines, and damage to their reputation. Understanding key laws helps you understand how businesses operate.

Employment Law

Minimum Wage: The government sets the minimum wage that employers must pay. This protects workers but increases costs for businesses, especially labour intensive industries like hospitality.

Working Hours: The Working Time Regulations limit the hours employees can work. Workers are entitled to rest breaks and holidays. This protects workers' wellbeing but means businesses must hire more staff.

Discrimination: The Equality Act 2010 makes it illegal to discriminate against employees based on age, gender, race, disability, religion, or sexual orientation. Businesses must have fair recruitment and promotion processes.

Health and Safety: The Health and Safety at Work Act requires businesses to provide safe working conditions. This includes proper equipment, training, and risk assessments. Breaches can result in large fines and criminal charges.

Unfair Dismissal: Employees cannot be sacked without good reason. After two years of employment, workers can claim unfair dismissal. Businesses must follow proper procedures.

Consumer Protection Law

Sale of Goods Act: Goods must be fit for purpose, of satisfactory quality, and match their description. If they are not, consumers can request a refund or replacement within a reasonable time.

Consumer Rights Act: Services must also be delivered with reasonable care and skill. This protects consumers who use services like hairdressing, plumbing, or legal advice.

Trade Descriptions Act: Advertisements must not be false or misleading. If a business claims a product does something it does not, this is illegal. This prevents deceptive marketing.

Competition Law

Competition law prevents businesses from unfairly dominating markets. It is illegal for businesses to collude (work together to fix prices or divide markets). The Competition and Markets Authority (CMA) investigates breaches. Large fines can be imposed on businesses that break competition law.

Impact of Legislation on Businesses

Costs
  • Higher wage bills (minimum wage)
  • Health and safety measures
  • Training and compliance costs
  • Legal advice
  • Potential fines for breaches
Benefits
  • Improved reputation
  • Better employee retention
  • Lower staff turnover costs
  • Builds consumer trust
  • Avoids legal problems
KEY TERM
Minimum Wage

Lowest hourly rate employers must pay workers by law

KEY TERM
Consumer Rights

Legal protections for buyers regarding quality and fairness

EXAM TIP

When discussing legislation, always link it to a specific business scenario. For example: If the minimum wage increases, a supermarket would face higher labour costs. They might respond by reducing staff hours or automating more checkout processes. Show how the law creates challenges and opportunities.

REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE
Apprentice Minimum Wage

In 2021, the apprentice minimum wage was significantly lower than the adult minimum wage. This encouraged businesses like Tesco and McDonald's to hire more apprentices. However, there is concern this could reduce opportunities for young adults in their 20s. This shows how legislation has unintended consequences.

Key Takeaways

  • Employment law sets minimum standards for wages, hours, health and safety, and fair treatment
  • Consumer protection law ensures goods and services meet quality standards
  • Competition law prevents businesses from unfairly dominating markets
  • Compliance costs money but builds reputation and avoids legal problems
6.3
The Competitive Environment
How businesses compete and respond to competition

What is Competition?

Competition is when businesses fight for customers and market share. Most markets have multiple competitors. Understanding competition helps you see how businesses must constantly innovate and improve to survive.

Competition occurs when businesses selling similar products or services try to attract the same customers. In a competitive market, no single business can control prices or supply. Customers have choices and can switch to competitors if they are unhappy.

Types of Competitive Markets

Perfect Competition: Many firms sell identical products. Prices are determined by supply and demand, not individual firms. In reality, this rarely exists. Example: wheat farming.

Monopolistic Competition: Many firms sell similar but slightly different products. Each firm has some control over price through brand differentiation. Example: high street coffee shops (Starbucks, Costa, Nero).

Oligopoly: A few large firms dominate the market. They compete intensely but watch each other carefully. Example: supermarkets (Tesco, Sainsbury's, Asda, Morrisons).

Monopoly: One firm dominates the entire market with no real competitors. Prices can be high because customers have no choice. Example: water companies in specific regions.

How Competition Affects Businesses

Price

In competitive markets, businesses cannot charge high prices or customers switch to rivals. Competition pushes prices down. This forces businesses to be efficient to maintain profits.

Quality

To win customers, businesses must offer good quality. Poor quality products lead to complaints and lost sales. Continuous improvement is essential in competitive markets.

Innovation

Businesses develop new products and services to stay ahead of rivals. Innovation can be product based (better features) or process based (more efficient manufacturing).

Marketing

Businesses invest heavily in marketing and branding to differentiate themselves. Advertising, social media, and customer service become crucial competitive tools.

How Businesses Respond to Competition

Price Competition: Offering lower prices than rivals. This attracts price sensitive customers but can hurt profit margins.

Non Price Competition: Competing on quality, brand, service, and location rather than price. Examples: Nike competes on brand reputation, Waitrose on quality, Domino's on delivery speed.

Differentiation: Making products unique so they are not directly comparable to rivals. Apple differentiates through design and brand loyalty. Coca Cola differentiates through taste and marketing.

Collaboration: Sometimes rivals work together on common interests like environmental standards. This can benefit everyone without illegal collusion.

KEY TERM
Competition

Businesses fighting for customers and market share in the same market

KEY TERM
Monopoly

A market with only one dominant firm that controls prices and supply

KEY TERM
Differentiation

Making a product unique or distinct from competitors

EXAM TIP

You might be asked: How does increased competition affect a business? Answer: Competition forces businesses to lower prices, improve quality, innovate, and invest in marketing. Weaker businesses may fail. Customers benefit from lower prices and better products. The industry becomes more efficient overall.

REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE
The Smartphone Market

The smartphone market has intense competition between Apple, Samsung, Google, and others. This drives constant innovation (better cameras, faster processors, bigger screens). Prices have fallen significantly over 10 years. Consumers benefit from incredible technology at lower prices. The intense competition means businesses must spend billions on research and development or they fall behind.

Key Takeaways

  • Competition varies from perfect competition to monopoly
  • Competition drives lower prices, higher quality, and innovation
  • Businesses use price and non price strategies to compete
  • Despite risks, entrepreneurs compete because profit opportunities exist
6.4
Environmental and Ethical Issues
Ethics, sustainability, and social responsibility

Business Ethics and Environmental Responsibility

Business ethics are the moral principles that guide how a business operates. Environmental issues relate to how businesses affect the planet. Both are increasingly important because consumers, employees, and governments care about these issues.

What is Business Ethics?

Business ethics are standards of right and wrong behaviour. An ethical business makes decisions that are honest and fair, even when it costs money. An unethical business cuts corners, misleads customers, or exploits workers for profit.

Ethical Considerations

Fair Trade: Fair trade ensures producers (especially in developing countries) receive fair prices for their goods. Fair trade chocolate and coffee are grown in conditions that respect worker rights and protect the environment. Consumers pay higher prices to support fair trade, but workers earn better incomes.

Animal Testing: Some cosmetics and medicines are tested on animals. Animal rights groups argue this is cruel and unnecessary. Many companies have switched to alternatives like computer testing. Some companies proudly advertise they are cruelty free to attract ethical consumers.

Working Conditions: Workers in some countries are paid very low wages to make clothes, shoes, or electronics for Western companies. Nike and Apple have faced criticism for poor working conditions in factories. Companies now publish reports on supply chain conditions and work to improve standards.

Tax Avoidance: Some large multinational companies use complex structures to minimize tax. While legal, this is seen as unethical by many because less money funds public services. Public pressure has led to greater scrutiny of tax practices.

Environmental Issues

Pollution: Factories produce air and water pollution. Manufacturing, transportation, and energy use create emissions. Businesses are increasingly required to measure and reduce their environmental impact.

Waste: Businesses generate enormous amounts of waste. Some is recycled, but much ends up in landfills. Plastic waste particularly concerns people. Companies like Starbucks have reduced single use cups to combat waste.

Carbon Footprint: The carbon footprint is the total greenhouse gas emissions from a business's activities. Flying, shipping, manufacturing, and packaging all contribute. Businesses are increasingly measuring and trying to reduce their carbon footprints because consumers care about climate change.

Sustainability: Sustainability means meeting present needs without harming the ability of future generations to meet their needs. Sustainable businesses use renewable resources, minimize waste, and reduce environmental damage. Examples: renewable energy, recycled materials, responsible logging.

Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR)

CSR is a concept where businesses take responsibility for their impact on society and the environment. It goes beyond legal requirements. Examples include supporting local charities, sponsoring youth programs, donating to disaster relief, environmental clean up projects, fair wages and safe conditions, and transparent reporting.

Costs and Benefits of Ethics and Sustainability

Costs
  • Renewable energy more expensive upfront
  • Fairer wages increase labour costs
  • Sustainable materials costly
  • Environmental monitoring expensive
  • Lower profits in short term
Benefits
  • Better brand reputation
  • Attracts conscious consumers
  • Attracts quality employees
  • Reduces waste and costs long term
  • Avoids legal problems and fines

Pressure Groups

Pressure groups are organizations that campaign for change. Environmental groups like Greenpeace and animal welfare groups like PETA pressure businesses to improve their practices. They use tactics like campaigns, boycotts, and public awareness.

When Greenpeace targets a company's practices through media campaigns, the company's reputation suffers and sales may decline. This motivates businesses to improve. Social media has amplified the power of pressure groups because negative stories spread quickly.

KEY TERM
Business Ethics

Moral principles that guide how businesses should operate

KEY TERM
Sustainability

Meeting present needs without harming future generations

KEY TERM
CSR

Corporate Social Responsibility: businesses taking responsibility for social and environmental impact

EXAM TIP

When discussing ethics, use specific examples. Do not just say being ethical is good. Explain specific choices (e.g., A clothing brand could use cheap overseas labour for high profits, but instead pays fair wages. This reduces profits but builds reputation and customer loyalty.) Show the trade off between short term profit and long term sustainability.

REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE
Patagonia

Patagonia (outdoor clothing brand) is famous for its strong commitment to environmental sustainability. They use recycled materials, pay fair wages, and give 1% of profits to environmental causes. They discourage fast fashion and encourage customers to repair products. They make less profit than competitors but have fierce brand loyalty and attract ethical consumers willing to pay premium prices.

Key Takeaways

  • Business ethics guide moral decisions; environmental issues affect the planet
  • Fair trade, animal testing, working conditions, and tax practices raise ethical questions
  • Sustainability and CSR require balancing profit with social and environmental responsibility
  • Pressure groups can force businesses to improve ethical and environmental practices
6.5
Globalisation
How businesses operate internationally

What is Globalisation?

Globalisation is the process where businesses, trade, culture, and ideas spread across borders. Technology and free trade have made the world connected. Most businesses today are affected by global forces and compete internationally.

Globalisation means businesses can operate worldwide, supply chains stretch across continents, and customers buy products from any country. Google operates in 190 countries. Nike manufactures in 40 countries. Amazon sells to 185 countries. This would not be possible without globalisation.

Reasons for Globalisation

Technology: The internet allows instant communication and electronic payments. Businesses can manage operations worldwide in real time. Video conferencing replaces business travel.

Transport: Cargo ships, aircraft, and trucks have improved. Shipping costs have fallen. Products can be transported efficiently across the world.

Trade Agreements: Countries have reduced tariffs and trade barriers. The World Trade Organization (WTO) promotes free trade. This makes it easier for businesses to export and import.

The Internet: E commerce allows businesses to sell directly to customers worldwide. Amazon, eBay, and Alibaba operate globally. Social media spreads brands across borders instantly.

Cost Reduction: Businesses can find cheaper materials and labour in developing countries. This reduces costs and increases profits.

Benefits of Globalisation for Businesses

Market Access

Businesses reach customers in new countries. A UK fashion brand can sell to millions in China or Brazil. This increases revenue and growth potential dramatically.

Cost Savings

Labour is cheaper in some countries. Manufacturing in Vietnam is cheaper than in the UK. Businesses can source raw materials from the cheapest suppliers globally.

Supply Chain

Businesses can split production across multiple countries. Each country specializes in what it does best. This creates efficient global supply chains.

Competition

Access to global suppliers and talent. Businesses find skilled workers anywhere in the world. This drives innovation and efficiency.

Drawbacks of Globalisation for Businesses

Competition

Businesses face competition from companies in other countries. An Indian software company competes with a US company for global contracts. This intensifies competition and can lower prices and profits.

Cultural Differences

Marketing and products must be adapted for different cultures and languages. What works in the UK might not work in Japan. This requires additional costs and expertise.

Currency Risk

Exchanging currencies between countries creates risk. If the pound weakens against the dollar, exports become cheaper but imports cost more. This uncertainty complicates planning.

Legal Issues

Different countries have different laws. A business must comply with laws in every country it operates. This increases complexity and cost.

Benefits and Drawbacks for Countries

Benefits for Countries: Foreign investment creates jobs. New technology and skills spread to developing countries. Trade increases national income. Consumer choice improves as products from around the world become available.

Drawbacks for Countries: Local businesses may not compete with global corporations. Jobs move from high wage countries to low wage countries (manufacturing moved from the UK to Asia). Cultural traditions may disappear as global brands dominate. Environmental damage can increase as corporations seek cheap labour in countries with weak regulations.

How Businesses Enter Foreign Markets

Exporting: The simplest method. Businesses produce at home and sell to foreign customers. Low risk but limited control in foreign markets. Example: UK wine exported to the US.

Licensing: A business allows another company to use its brand/technology in exchange for payment. Minimal investment but loses control of quality. Example: Nike licensing its brand to shoe manufacturers in China.

Joint Ventures: Two businesses from different countries partner to create a new business. Shares risk and investment. Example: A UK bank partnering with a Chinese bank to operate in China.

Franchising: A business sells the right to use its brand and methods to independent operators. McDonald's franchises worldwide. Low investment for the parent company but requires careful control of standards.

KEY TERM
Globalisation

Process where businesses and trade spread across international borders

KEY TERM
Export

Selling goods produced domestically to foreign customers

KEY TERM
Franchise

Right to use a business's brand and methods in a specific location

EXAM TIP

When asked about globalisation, remember it has both positives and negatives. Do not say it is good or bad. Explain specific impacts: Globalisation has allowed Nike to reduce costs by manufacturing in Vietnam, increasing profits. However, it has also caused UK manufacturing jobs to disappear, hurting local communities.

REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE
Apple's Global Supply Chain

Apple designs iPhones in the US, manufactures components in various countries (Japan, South Korea, Taiwan), assembles them in China, and sells them in 185 countries. This global supply chain is efficient but creates dependency on multiple countries. When COVID 19 disrupted shipping, iPhone production slowed worldwide, showing how globalisation creates interconnected risk.

Key Takeaways

  • Globalisation driven by technology, transport, trade agreements, and the internet
  • Businesses access new markets, reduce costs, and tap global talent
  • Globalisation intensifies competition and creates legal complexity
  • Entry methods include exporting, licensing, joint ventures, and franchising
6.6
Multinational Companies
Global corporations and their impact

What is a Multinational Company?

Multinational Companies (MNCs) are businesses that operate in multiple countries. They are huge, powerful, and shape global markets. Understanding MNCs helps you see how globalisation really works.

An MNC is a company that owns and operates businesses in two or more countries. Most MNCs are based in developed countries (US, UK, Japan, Germany) but operate in developing countries too. Examples: Apple, Google, Mercedes Benz, Unilever, Nestlé, McDonald's, Samsung, Toyota.

Benefits of MNCs to Host Countries

Employment

MNCs create millions of jobs globally. In developing countries, factory jobs provide income that improves living standards. Example: Nike factories in Vietnam employ hundreds of thousands of workers.

Investment

MNCs bring foreign direct investment (money invested in local businesses and infrastructure). This builds factories, roads, power plants, and telecommunications networks that benefit the entire country.

Technology

MNCs introduce new technology and skills to developing countries. Workers learn from advanced processes. Local suppliers upgrade their capabilities to meet MNC standards.

Tax Revenue

MNCs pay taxes to host governments. This funds schools, hospitals, roads, and other public services. Tax revenue helps governments develop their countries.

Drawbacks of MNCs to Host Countries

Exploitation

MNCs sometimes pay very low wages to exploit poor workers. Working conditions may be unsafe. Workers have little power to demand better treatment because jobs are scarce. However, even low MNC wages are often better than local alternatives.

Environmental Damage

MNCs may pollute air and water in host countries. They locate in developing countries partly because environmental regulations are weak. Developing countries bear the environmental cost while MNCs profit. Example: Mining companies leaving polluted land.

Tax Avoidance

Large MNCs use complex structures to minimize taxes. They shift profits to countries with low tax rates. While legal, this means host governments receive less tax revenue. Developing countries are harmed most because they need tax revenue badly.

Cultural Impact

MNCs spread global brands and culture, replacing local traditions. McDonald's, Starbucks, and American movies dominate globally. Local cultural businesses struggle to compete. Some see this as cultural imperialism.

Benefits and Drawbacks for the MNC

Benefits for the MNC: Access to new markets and customers. Lower production costs in developing countries increase profits. Access to natural resources. Spread risk across multiple countries and markets. Tax advantages through global structures.

Drawbacks for the MNC: Managing operations in different countries is complex and expensive. Currency fluctuations affect profits. Political instability and war can disrupt operations. Different laws and regulations in each country complicate business. Cultural differences require local adaptation.

KEY TERM
Multinational Company

Business that owns and operates in multiple countries

KEY TERM
Foreign Direct Investment

Money invested by businesses in foreign countries

KEY TERM
Host Country

Country where a foreign business operates

EXAM TIP

MNC questions often ask about impacts on developing countries. Remember: benefits (jobs, investment, technology, tax) are real but so are drawbacks (exploitation, environmental damage, tax avoidance, cultural loss). A balanced answer acknowledges both sides and evaluates whether benefits outweigh drawbacks (or vice versa).

REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE
Samsung in Vietnam

Samsung operates in South Korea but manufactures and sells globally. In Vietnam, Samsung factories employ 200,000+ workers. This provides jobs and income but workers earn low wages for long hours. Samsung pays taxes to the Vietnamese government but also lobbies for tax breaks. The technology transfer has helped Vietnam develop, but environmental pollution from factories affects local communities.

Key Takeaways

  • MNCs operate in multiple countries and are powerful global forces
  • Benefits to host countries: jobs, investment, technology, tax revenue
  • Drawbacks to host countries: exploitation, environmental damage, tax avoidance, cultural loss
  • MNCs benefit from lower costs and new markets but face complexity and risk
6.7
Exchange Rates
Currency values and their business impact

What is an Exchange Rate?

An exchange rate is the price of one currency in terms of another. The pound to dollar rate tells you how many dollars you get for one pound. Exchange rates change constantly and affect international trade. Understanding them helps you see how global business works.

The exchange rate between the pound and dollar might be 1 pound equals 1.25 dollars. This means if you exchange 100 pounds, you get 125 dollars. Exchange rates are determined by supply and demand for currencies. If many people want pounds, the pound strengthens (appreciates). If few want pounds, the pound weakens (depreciates).

Strong Currency vs Weak Currency

Strong Currency: When the pound is strong, one pound buys more foreign currency. British goods seem expensive to foreign buyers. But imports become cheaper. A strong pound helps importers but hurts exporters.

Weak Currency: When the pound is weak, one pound buys less foreign currency. British goods seem cheap to foreign buyers. But imports become expensive. A weak pound helps exporters but hurts importers.

Impact on Businesses

Exporters: UK companies selling abroad are affected by exchange rates. If the pound strengthens, British products become more expensive to foreign customers. Sales may fall. If the pound weakens, UK goods become cheaper and more competitive. Sales may increase.

Importers: UK companies buying foreign materials are affected oppositely. If the pound strengthens, foreign materials become cheaper (good). If the pound weakens, foreign materials become more expensive (bad).

Revenue and Costs: A UK exporter earning euros faces risk. If the euro falls against the pound, the same euro revenue means less pounds earned. This reduces profits even if sales remain the same.

How Businesses Respond to Exchange Rate Changes

Hedging: Businesses use financial instruments (like forward contracts) to lock in exchange rates. If a company knows it will receive euros in 3 months, it can agree today on the pound exchange rate. This removes uncertainty.

Changing Prices: If the pound weakens, exporters might not lower prices because their foreign revenue remains valuable. If the pound strengthens, exporters might lower prices to remain competitive.

Moving Production: If the pound stays strong, exporters might move production to countries with weak currencies to reduce costs. This is a long term response.

Marketing: Companies emphasize quality and brand to justify prices rather than compete on price when the pound is strong.

UK Examples

When Brexit occurred in 2016, the pound fell significantly against the dollar and euro. UK exporters like Rolls Royce (aircraft engines) and Diageo (whisky) benefited because their products became cheaper abroad. Sales increased. However, UK importers suffered because raw materials and components from overseas became more expensive.

KEY TERM
Exchange Rate

Price of one currency expressed in terms of another currency

KEY TERM
Strong Currency

Currency that buys more foreign currency; makes exports expensive

KEY TERM
Weak Currency

Currency that buys less foreign currency; makes exports cheaper

KEY TERM
Appreciate

When a currency increases in value against other currencies

KEY TERM
Depreciate

When a currency decreases in value against other currencies

EXAM TIP

Exchange rate questions typically ask how a change affects a specific business. Always identify: (1) Is this business an exporter or importer? (2) Is the pound getting stronger or weaker? (3) What is the impact? Example: A strong pound helps a UK importer of Japanese cars because the yen becomes cheaper relative to the pound, reducing costs.

REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE
ASDA and Exchange Rates

ASDA (UK supermarket) imports goods globally. When the pound weakened in 2016, their import costs increased significantly. ASDA had to raise prices in stores or accept lower profits. Meanwhile, UK agricultural exporters (beef, cheese) benefited because their products became more competitive in EU markets at the lower pound rate.

Key Takeaways

  • Exchange rates determine the price of one currency in terms of another
  • Strong currency hurts exporters but helps importers
  • Weak currency helps exporters but hurts importers
  • Businesses manage exchange rate risk through hedging, pricing, and strategic decisions
