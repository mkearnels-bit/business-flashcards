Understanding Business Activity
1.1
Purpose of Business Activity
What a business is and how it creates value

What is a Business?

A business is an organisation that produces or sells goods and services to customers. The main purpose is to meet customer needs and wants. Businesses combine resources (land, labour, capital, enterprise) to create goods or services that people will buy.

Needs and Wants

Needs are things people must have to survive, like food, water, and shelter. Wants are things people would like to have but do not need to survive, like holidays, smartphones, and fashion items. Businesses produce both.

KEY TERM
Adding Value

The difference between the selling price of a product and the cost of the materials and components used to make it. Adding value is how businesses make profit.

Ways Businesses Add Value

  • Branding and Image: Creating a strong brand makes customers willing to pay more. For example, Nike charges more than unknown brands because of its brand reputation.
  • Quality: Higher quality products cost more. Customers pay premium prices for durability and performance.
  • Design: Attractive, innovative design increases the value customers perceive. Apple products are priced high partly due to design.
  • Convenience: Making products easy to buy or use adds value. Delivery services, mobile apps, and extended opening hours add value.
  • Unique Selling Proposition (USP): A feature that makes a product different from competitors. This allows businesses to charge higher prices.

Goods and Services

Goods are physical products you can touch, like cars, clothes, and food. Services are intangible products where customers buy the benefit or experience, like haircuts, banking, and entertainment. Many businesses provide both.

Factors of Production

All businesses use four main factors of production to create goods and services:

  • Land: Natural resources like soil, minerals, water, and forests. Used in agriculture, mining, and manufacturing.
  • Labour: The work and effort of people. Includes manual and intellectual work.
  • Capital: Money and machinery used to produce goods. Includes buildings, equipment, and technology.
  • Enterprise: The skill and risk-taking of entrepreneurs who organise the other three factors to create business.

Opportunity Cost

Opportunity cost is the value of the next best alternative when making a choice. If a business uses its money to buy machinery, the opportunity cost is what else it could have done with that money (like buying new vehicles or hiring more staff). Understanding opportunity cost helps businesses make better decisions.

Specialisation

Specialisation means focusing on what you do best. A business may specialise in one product or service. A person may specialise in a specific skill. Specialisation increases efficiency and quality because people become very skilled at one thing.

REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE
Apple's Value Addition

Apple adds value through brand, design, and user experience. A smartphone costs Apple around 200 pounds to manufacture but sells for 800 pounds. The 600 pound difference comes from brand reputation, elegant design, and the ecosystem that makes Apple products easy to use.

EXAM TIP

When asked about adding value, always give specific examples. You might be asked to analyse a case study and identify how a business adds value. Remember that adding value is how businesses make profit and survive.

Key Takeaways

  • A business produces goods and services to meet customer needs and wants
  • Needs are essentials; wants are desires
  • Adding value is the difference between selling price and material costs
  • Businesses add value through branding, quality, design, convenience, and unique selling points
  • Factors of production are land, labour, capital, and enterprise
  • Opportunity cost is the value of the next best alternative
  • Specialisation increases efficiency and quality
1.2
Classification of Businesses
Primary, secondary, and tertiary sectors

The Three Sectors of the Economy

The economy is divided into three sectors based on what businesses do. Understanding these sectors helps explain how economies develop and change.

Primary Sector

The primary sector extracts raw materials directly from nature. Activities include farming, fishing, mining, forestry, and oil extraction. Products include crops, fish, metals, timber, and oil. This sector is essential because it provides the raw materials other sectors need.

Primary sector businesses tend to be affected by weather, seasons, and natural events. Employment in this sector is usually declining in developed countries but growing in developing countries.

Secondary Sector

The secondary sector manufactures raw materials into finished goods. Activities include car production, food processing, textile manufacturing, and construction. The secondary sector adds significant value by transforming primary products into goods people want to buy. A tree (primary) becomes furniture (secondary).

Tertiary Sector

The tertiary sector provides services rather than goods. Activities include retail, healthcare, education, entertainment, banking, transport, and hospitality. This sector has grown dramatically in developed countries. Employment in services is now the largest in countries like the UK and USA.

KEY TERM
Quaternary Sector

Some economists add a fourth sector for knowledge-based services like research, design, and IT. These are high-value services that require specialised skills.

Sector What They Do Examples
Primary Extract raw materials from nature Farming, fishing, mining, forestry, oil extraction
Secondary Manufacture goods from raw materials Car production, food processing, textiles, construction
Tertiary Provide services to customers Retail, healthcare, education, entertainment, banking, transport

Why the Balance Changes

As countries develop, the balance between sectors shifts. Developing countries have large primary sectors because people still need basic resources. Many people work in agriculture. Developed countries have small primary sectors and large tertiary sectors. Economies become service-based as people have more money to spend on services and manufacturing becomes automated.

Public and Private Sectors

Businesses can also be classified as public or private. Private sector businesses are owned by individuals or groups and aim to make profit. Public sector organisations are owned and run by government to provide services like education, healthcare, and policing. Mixed economy countries have both private and public sector organisations.

REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE
UK's Changing Economy

In 1900, most UK workers were in agriculture and manufacturing. Today, over 80% work in services like retail, healthcare, education, and banking. This shift reflects economic development and shows how successful economies become service-based.

EXAM TIP

Questions often ask you to classify businesses into sectors. Be specific with examples. Know why the balance between sectors changes as countries develop. You might need to analyse how a country's economic structure affects businesses.

Key Takeaways

  • Primary sector extracts raw materials from nature
  • Secondary sector manufactures goods from raw materials
  • Tertiary sector provides services
  • Developing countries have large primary sectors; developed countries have large tertiary sectors
  • Private sector businesses aim for profit; public sector provides government services
  • Most developed economies are service-based
1.3
Enterprise and Entrepreneurship
Starting a business and taking risks

What is Enterprise?

Enterprise means recognising opportunities and taking action to create new businesses or develop new ideas. An entrepreneur is someone who starts and runs a business, taking on the risks in hope of making profit. Enterprise is the ability to spot market gaps and fill them.

KEY TERM
Entrepreneur

A person who starts a business, takes risks, and organises resources. Entrepreneurs combine the other factors of production (land, labour, capital) to create goods or services.

Characteristics of Entrepreneurs

  • Risk Taking: Entrepreneurs accept that their business might fail. They invest their own money and time with no guarantee of success.
  • Creativity: Entrepreneurs think of new ideas and new ways to do things. They spot market opportunities others miss.
  • Determination: Entrepreneurs persist when things get difficult. Starting a business involves many challenges and setbacks.
  • Hard Working: Successful entrepreneurs work long hours, especially in the early stages. They are committed and dedicated.
  • Problem Solving: Entrepreneurs find solutions to obstacles. They adapt and change plans when necessary.
  • Passion: Successful entrepreneurs care deeply about their business and what they are trying to achieve.

Reasons for Starting a Business

People start businesses for various reasons. Some want independence and to be their own boss. Others spot a market gap and see profit potential. Some need income and cannot find employment elsewhere. Some have innovative ideas they want to develop. Personal circumstances like redundancy sometimes force people to start their own business.

Risks and Rewards

Rewards of starting a business: Independence, potential to make large profits, personal satisfaction, flexibility to set own hours, and being able to develop your own ideas.

Risks of starting a business: Financial loss, uncertainty of income (especially at first), long working hours, stress and pressure, competition from other businesses, and the business might fail.

Government Support for Startups

Governments support new businesses because they create jobs and economic growth. Support includes business grants, low-interest loans, business advice and training, help with premises, and tax breaks. These reduce the financial risk for entrepreneurs and increase their chances of success.

Why Businesses Fail

Many new businesses fail. Common reasons include poor planning, under-capitalisation (not enough money), poor market research (not understanding customers), competition is stronger than expected, poor location, bad timing (bad economic conditions), poor management, and running out of cash.

REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE
Sara Blakely and Spanx

Sara Blakely saw a problem: underwear showed through clothing. She started Spanx with 5,000 pounds and developed a product that solved this problem. Her enterprise and creativity created a billion-pound business. She demonstrated risk-taking, determination, and problem-solving.

EXAM TIP

When discussing entrepreneurship, always balance risks and rewards. You might be asked why a business failed or why someone succeeded. Link this to characteristics of entrepreneurs and reasons for success or failure. Case studies often feature entrepreneurs.

Key Takeaways

  • Enterprise is recognising opportunities and taking action
  • Entrepreneurs are people who start and run businesses
  • Key characteristics are risk-taking, creativity, determination, hard work, and problem-solving
  • People start businesses for independence, profit, necessity, or to develop ideas
  • Rewards include independence and profit; risks include financial loss and stress
  • Governments support startups with grants, loans, advice, and tax breaks
  • Businesses fail due to poor planning, lack of capital, poor research, or bad management
1.4
Types of Business Organisation
Sole traders, partnerships, companies, and more

Business Structures

Businesses are organized in different ways depending on ownership, size, and legal requirements. The structure affects how much owners can profit and how much personal liability they have.

Sole Trader

A sole trader is a business owned by one person. The owner runs the business and keeps all profits but is personally responsible for any losses. Sole traders are common among small businesses like shops, plumbing, hairdressing, and consulting. They are easy and cheap to set up but have unlimited liability, meaning if the business fails, the owner's personal assets (house, car) can be taken to pay debts.

Partnership

A partnership is a business owned by two or more people. Partners share ownership, responsibility, and profits. Partnerships share the workload and combine skills and capital. However, each partner has unlimited liability, so if one partner makes a bad decision, all partners are responsible.

Limited Company (Ltd)

A Limited Company is owned by shareholders who have limited liability. This means they can only lose the money they invested. If the company fails, personal assets are protected. Most medium and large businesses are limited companies. However, they are more expensive and complex to set up, requiring registration with Companies House.

KEY TERM
Limited Liability

The owners' financial risk is limited to the amount they invested. Personal assets are protected if the business fails.

Public Limited Company (PLC)

A Public Limited Company is a larger limited company that sells shares to the general public on the stock exchange. Examples include Tesco, HSBC, and Rolls Royce. PLCs can raise large amounts of capital from share sales but face strict regulation and public scrutiny. Directors answer to shareholders.

Franchises

A franchise is where a business (franchisor) allows another person (franchisee) to use its name, products, and systems. The franchisee pays fees and follows the franchisor's rules. Examples include McDonald's, Subway, and Costa Coffee. Franchises reduce risk because the business model is proven, but franchisees have less independence and pay ongoing fees.

Not-for-Profit Organisations

Not-for-profit organisations exist to provide a service or support a cause, not to make profit. Examples include charities, schools, hospitals, and sports clubs. Any profit made is reinvested into the organisation to improve services. They may receive donations, grants, and funding from government.

Type Ownership Liability Advantages
Sole Trader One person Unlimited Easy to set up, keep all profit, independent
Partnership Two or more Unlimited Share workload, combine skills, more capital
Ltd Company Shareholders Limited Personal assets protected, can raise capital
PLC Public shareholders Limited Large capital available, professional image
Franchise Franchisee and franchisor Limited (usually) Proven business model, brand recognition

Choosing the Right Structure

Choosing a business structure depends on several factors: the amount of capital needed, how much personal risk the owner wants to take, how many owners there are, whether the business will grow, and tax implications. A small startup might be a sole trader, but as it grows, the owner might become a limited company for personal protection.

REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE
Business Structure Evolution

Many businesses start as sole traders with one person running everything. If they succeed and grow, they might take on a partner (partnership), then incorporate as a limited company to protect personal assets and enable expansion. This is a typical progression as businesses develop.

EXAM TIP

Know the advantages and disadvantages of each structure. You might be asked to recommend a structure for a specific situation. Always consider factors like capital needed, liability protection, and growth plans. Understand the difference between unlimited and limited liability clearly.

Key Takeaways

  • Sole traders are owned by one person with unlimited liability
  • Partnerships share ownership and liability
  • Limited companies offer owners limited liability protection
  • PLCs are large companies with shares sold to the public
  • Franchises allow others to use a proven business model
  • Not-for-profit organisations exist to serve a cause, not make profit
  • Business structure choice depends on capital, liability, ownership, and growth plans
1.5
Business Aims and Objectives
What businesses are trying to achieve

Aims and Objectives Explained

Aims are the general long-term goals of a business. Objectives are specific, measurable targets that help the business achieve its aims. For example, a business might have the aim of growth, with an objective to increase sales by 20% in the next year.

KEY TERM
Objective

A specific, measurable target that a business wants to achieve, usually with a timeframe. Objectives are specific and can be measured.

Common Business Objectives

  • Survival: Especially for new businesses, the primary objective is to stay in business, make enough revenue to pay costs, and avoid failure.
  • Profit: Making money is central to most private businesses. Profit maximisation or satisfactory profit levels are common objectives.
  • Growth: Increasing sales, market share, or the number of locations. Growth provides security and increases power in the market.
  • Market Share: Increasing the percentage of the market a business controls. This shows competitiveness and market position.
  • Customer Satisfaction: Building loyalty and reputation by providing quality products and good service. Satisfied customers return and recommend the business.
  • Social or Ethical Objectives: Some businesses prioritise environmental sustainability, fair wages, community support, or ethical practices.

How Objectives Change

Business objectives change as the business grows. A new startup focuses on survival and establishing itself. Once stable, a business might focus on growth and profit. A mature, large business might focus on market share, innovation, and social responsibility. Economic conditions also affect objectives. During a recession, survival becomes the focus. In good times, growth becomes possible.

Private Sector vs Public Sector Objectives

Private sector businesses usually prioritise profit and growth. They exist to generate returns for owners and shareholders. Public sector organisations prioritise providing quality services and meeting public need. They are funded by government and taxes, not through profit. Their objectives include accessibility, quality, and cost-efficiency.

SMART Objectives

Effective objectives are often SMART: Specific (clear about what is to be achieved), Measurable (can be quantified with numbers), Achievable (realistic), Relevant (connected to the business), and Time-bound (have a deadline). For example, "Increase online sales by 30% within 12 months" is SMART.

REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE
Netflix's Changing Objectives

Netflix started with the aim of disrupting video rental (survival and market entry). As it grew, objectives shifted to global expansion and then to content creation and quality. Today, Netflix balances growth, profitability, and subscriber satisfaction. Its objectives reflect its stage in business development.

EXAM TIP

Understand how to set SMART objectives. You might be asked to evaluate objectives or explain why objectives change. Know the difference between private and public sector aims. Questions might ask how economic conditions affect objectives.

Key Takeaways

  • Aims are general long-term goals; objectives are specific, measurable targets
  • Common objectives are survival, profit, growth, market share, and customer satisfaction
  • Social and ethical objectives are becoming increasingly important
  • Objectives change as businesses grow and conditions change
  • Private sector focuses on profit; public sector focuses on service
  • SMART objectives are specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound
1.6
Stakeholders
Everyone affected by a business and their interests

Who are Stakeholders?

Stakeholders are people and groups who are affected by or have an interest in a business. They are not always owners. Stakeholders include employees, customers, suppliers, the local community, government, and owners. Each stakeholder has different interests and priorities.

KEY TERM
Stakeholder

Any person or group who is affected by a business or has an interest in its success or failure.

Internal Stakeholders

  • Owners: Want maximum profit and business growth. They take financial risk and gain rewards.
  • Employees: Want good wages, job security, safe working conditions, and opportunities for development. They depend on the business for income.

External Stakeholders

  • Customers: Want good quality products at fair prices. They depend on the business meeting their needs.
  • Suppliers: Want reliable orders and prompt payment. They depend on the business as a customer.
  • Local Community: Want minimal negative impact (pollution, noise, traffic). They may benefit from employment and investment but worry about effects.
  • Government: Wants tax revenue, employment, and compliance with laws. They regulate business through health and safety laws, environmental rules, and employment law.
  • Lenders: Banks and other lenders want repayment with interest. They have interest in business success.

Stakeholder Conflict

Stakeholders have conflicting interests. Owners want high profit, but employees want higher wages. Customers want low prices, but owners want high profit. The community wants environmental protection, but factories may cause pollution. Government wants tax revenue, but businesses want lower taxes. Businesses must balance these conflicting interests.

Managing Stakeholder Interests

Businesses manage stakeholder interests through communication, negotiation, and compromise. Listening to stakeholders helps prevent problems. Transparent communication builds trust. Some businesses use stakeholder committees where representatives of different groups meet regularly. Ethical practices and social responsibility show commitment to stakeholders beyond just profit.

REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE
Football Club Stakeholders

A football club has multiple stakeholders: fans want winning teams, employees want secure jobs, owners want profit from match days and merchandise, the local community benefits from employment and pride, and lenders want repayment. Balancing these needs is complex, especially when the club is struggling financially.

EXAM TIP

Questions often ask you to identify stakeholders and their interests in a case study. Remember that stakeholders can have conflicting objectives. You might be asked how a business decision affects different stakeholders. Always consider multiple perspectives.

Key Takeaways

  • Stakeholders are people and groups affected by or interested in a business
  • Internal stakeholders are owners and employees
  • External stakeholders include customers, suppliers, community, government, and lenders
  • Stakeholders have different and often conflicting objectives
  • Businesses must balance stakeholder interests through communication and compromise
  • Managing stakeholder relationships is important for long-term business success
1.7
Business Growth and Size
How businesses grow and the benefits of scale

Measuring Business Size

Business size can be measured in several ways. Number of employees: How many people work for the business. Revenue (turnover): Total sales income. Market share: What percentage of the market the business controls. Capital employed: Total money invested in the business. Large businesses have more employees, higher revenue, greater market share, and more capital invested.

Types of Business Growth

Internal (organic) growth happens when a business expands from within. A shop opens more locations, a manufacturer builds new factories, or a business launches new products. This growth is financed from profits or by borrowing. It is slower but the business stays independent.

External growth happens through mergers and takeovers. A merger is when two businesses join together as equals. A takeover is when one business buys another. External growth is faster but can be costly and may cause disruption.

Why Some Businesses Stay Small

Not all businesses want to grow. Some owners prefer independence and control. Growing requires capital investment and hiring more staff, which creates complexity. Small businesses can serve local markets effectively. Some products or services work best as small operations. The owner's lifestyle preferences matter.

Economies of Scale

KEY TERM
Economies of Scale

The cost advantages that a business gains as it increases in size. As output grows, the average cost per unit falls because fixed costs are spread over more units and the business can benefit from bulk buying, specialised equipment, and other efficiencies.

As businesses grow, their average cost per unit produced falls. Larger businesses can negotiate better prices with suppliers (purchasing economies), spread marketing costs across more products (marketing economies), invest in specialised machinery (technical economies), borrow at lower interest rates (financial economies), and employ specialists (managerial economies). This gives large businesses significant cost advantages over smaller competitors.

Economies and Diseconomies of Scale Interactive
The U-shaped curve shows average costs at different output levels

Diseconomies of Scale

As businesses grow very large, average costs can start rising again. This is diseconomies of scale. Communication becomes slower with many layers of management. Decision-making slows down. Workers may feel less motivated in huge organisations. Managing a very large business becomes complex and expensive. Coordination problems increase. There is an optimal size where average costs are lowest.

Advantages of Growth

  • Lower average costs due to economies of scale
  • Greater market power and influence
  • Ability to invest in innovation and new technology
  • Can spread risk across more products or markets
  • Better able to attract and retain quality employees with career progression
  • Greater prestige and brand recognition

Disadvantages of Growth

  • Loss of flexibility and ability to adapt quickly
  • Loss of personal management and control
  • May lose original company culture
  • Diseconomies of scale if growth goes too far
  • Greater complexity in management
  • External growth can be expensive and involve risk
REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE
Amazon's Growth

Amazon grew through internal expansion (opening new fulfillment centers and expanding product range) and external growth (acquiring Whole Foods). Growth gave Amazon economies of scale, allowing lower prices, and greater market power. The company became dominant in online retail through continuous expansion.

EXAM TIP

Understand economies of scale clearly. You might be asked why large businesses have cost advantages or how growth affects costs. Know the difference between internal and external growth. Questions might ask whether a business should grow and why.

Key Takeaways

  • Business size is measured by employees, revenue, market share, or capital
  • Internal growth comes from within; external growth is through mergers and takeovers
  • Some businesses stay small by choice or due to market conditions
  • Economies of scale mean average costs fall as output increases
  • Five types of economies of scale: purchasing, marketing, technical, financial, managerial
  • Diseconomies of scale can occur when businesses grow too large
  • Growth has advantages (cost savings, market power) and disadvantages (complexity, loss of control)
1.8
Business Location
Factors affecting where businesses locate

Why Location Matters

A business's location affects its success. Location influences costs, revenue, and customer convenience. Poor location can mean fewer customers, higher transport costs, and failure. Good location attracts customers and reduces operational costs.

Key Location Factors

  • Proximity to Customers: Retail businesses need to be where customers are. City centre locations have high foot traffic. Online businesses are less affected.
  • Raw Materials: Manufacturers locate near raw materials to reduce transport costs. Steel mills are near iron ore deposits. Food processors are near agricultural areas.
  • Labour Supply: Businesses need workers nearby. They locate in areas with suitable skills or populations. Universities attract tech companies for graduates.
  • Transport and Infrastructure: Access to motorways, railways, and ports is crucial. Good transport reduces delivery times and costs. Industrial estates have good infrastructure.
  • Costs: Land, premises, and electricity costs vary by location. Businesses locate where costs are lowest, usually away from expensive city centres for factories.
  • Competition: Some businesses cluster together (high streets with multiple shops). Others avoid competition and locate where there are fewer rivals.
  • Government Incentives: Governments offer tax breaks, grants, and subsidies to attract businesses to specific areas. Enterprise zones have reduced business rates.

E-commerce and Location

E-commerce has changed location importance. Online retailers can be anywhere because customers order online. However, they need good warehouse locations near transport links for quick delivery. Customer location matters less for online retail but logistics locations matter more. E-commerce has reduced the importance of physical proximity to customers for many businesses.

Relocation

Some established businesses move location. Reasons include expanding beyond current premises, finding cheaper premises, being closer to customers or suppliers, accessing better labour, or escaping high costs. Relocation is expensive (moving equipment, disruption to business, retraining staff) but sometimes essential for growth or survival.

REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE
Silicon Valley Tech Growth

Tech companies clustered in Silicon Valley near Stanford University for access to graduates and skilled workers. This created an ecosystem where companies could hire talent, share knowledge, and attract investment. Good weather, venture capital availability, and a culture of entrepreneurship also attracted businesses to this location.

EXAM TIP

When analysing location decisions, consider the type of business. Retail, manufacturing, and services have different location needs. You might be asked why a business chose a location or where a new business should locate. Always justify your answer with relevant factors.

Key Takeaways

  • Location affects costs, revenue, and business success
  • Key factors are proximity to customers, raw materials, labour, transport, costs, competition, and government incentives
  • Different business types have different location needs
  • E-commerce has reduced the importance of customer proximity but increased logistics importance
  • Relocation is costly but sometimes necessary for growth
  • Clustering of similar businesses can create advantages through shared resources and knowledge

Business Planning
Creating plans and managing finances

What is a Business Plan?

A business plan is a detailed document that describes what a business will do and how it will achieve its objectives. It outlines the business idea, market research, marketing strategy, financial projections, and management structure. Business plans are used internally to guide decisions and externally to persuade lenders and investors.

Contents of a Business Plan

  • Executive Summary: A brief overview of the whole plan
  • Business Description: What the business does and its mission
  • Market Research: Analysis of the market, customers, and competition
  • Marketing Strategy: How the business will attract and keep customers
  • Financial Projections: Predicted revenue, costs, and profit
  • Funding Requirements: How much money is needed and what it will be used for
  • Management Structure: Who will run the business and what experience they have
  • Timescales: When objectives will be achieved

Why Business Plans Matter

Business plans help entrepreneurs think through their idea carefully. They identify problems and opportunities before starting. Banks and investors require detailed business plans before lending or investing money. Plans provide targets and milestones to track progress. Regular review of plans helps businesses adapt to changing conditions.

Limitations of Planning

Planning is based on assumptions that may be wrong. Market conditions change unexpectedly. Competition may be stronger than predicted. The economy may go into recession. Customer preferences change. Technology disrupts industries. Plans become outdated quickly in fast-changing markets. Plans also require time and resources to create. Entrepreneurs may focus too much on planning and not enough on actually starting the business.

Basic Financial Terms

Revenue (turnover): Total money received from selling products or services. Revenue = Price x Quantity Sold. If a shop sells 100 items at 10 pounds each, revenue is 1,000 pounds.

Costs: Money spent to run the business. Fixed costs (rent, insurance) stay the same regardless of output. Variable costs (raw materials, packaging) change with output. Total Costs = Fixed Costs + Variable Costs.

Profit: Money left after paying all costs. Profit = Revenue minus Total Costs. If a business has revenue of 10,000 pounds and costs of 7,000 pounds, profit is 3,000 pounds.

FORMULA

Revenue = Price x Quantity

Example: Price of 20 pounds x 500 units sold = 10,000 pounds revenue

FORMULA

Total Costs = Fixed Costs + Variable Costs

Example: Fixed costs 3,000 pounds + Variable costs 4,000 pounds = Total costs 7,000 pounds

FORMULA

Profit = Revenue minus Total Costs

Example: Revenue 10,000 pounds minus Total costs 7,000 pounds = Profit 3,000 pounds

Break Even

Break even is when revenue equals costs, so profit is zero. Businesses calculate how many units they need to sell to break even. This tells them the minimum sales needed to avoid loss. After break even, any additional sales contribute to profit.

REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE
Cafe Business Plan

A cafe business plan would include: cafe location and size, menu and pricing, customer market (students, commuters, professionals), marketing (social media, local ads), fixed costs (rent 2,000 pounds/month, salaries 4,000 pounds/month), variable costs (coffee, food 30% of revenue), projected revenue, and break even point (e.g., 500 customers/month at average 8 pounds). The plan guides start-up and early operations.

EXAM TIP

Know how to calculate revenue, costs, and profit. You will likely be asked to do calculations in the exam. Understand fixed and variable costs clearly. Questions might ask about break even or why a business plan is important. Practice calculating profit from given data.

Key Takeaways

  • A business plan is a detailed document guiding business operations and securing finance
  • Plans include description, market research, strategy, and financial projections
  • Plans help entrepreneurs think through ideas and enable lenders to assess risk
  • Plans have limitations and can become outdated quickly
  • Revenue = Price x Quantity; Total Costs = Fixed + Variable; Profit = Revenue minus Total Costs
  • Break even is when revenue equals costs
  • Regular planning and review helps businesses stay on track and adapt to change
