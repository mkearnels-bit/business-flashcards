Finance
5.1
The Need for Finance
Why businesses need money and what they use it for

Why Do Businesses Need Finance?

Every business needs money to operate. Finance is cash used to pay for what the business needs. Businesses need finance for two main reasons: to start up and to grow and expand.

KEY TERM
Finance

Money made available to a business for it to operate, expand, and grow.

Start-up Finance

A new business needs finance to buy everything needed before it can open. Start-up finance is used for:

  • Premises (rent deposits or purchasing property)
  • Equipment and machinery
  • Stock and inventory
  • Vehicles and delivery equipment
  • Furniture and fixtures
  • Marketing and advertising
  • Legal costs and licenses

Growth and Expansion Finance

Established businesses need additional finance to grow. They use it for:

  • Building new factories or shops
  • Buying new equipment or technology
  • Research and development of new products
  • Marketing new products or entering new markets
  • Taking over or merging with other businesses
  • Hiring more employees

Working Capital

Businesses also need money to cover day-to-day running costs, called working capital. This includes paying employees, suppliers, utilities, and rent each month.

EXAM TIP

Examiners often ask what finance is needed for. Always link your answer to the type of business and stage of growth.

REAL WORLD EXAMPLE

A startup pizza restaurant needs finance to rent a shop, buy an oven, purchase kitchen equipment, buy initial ingredients, decorate the space, and market the opening. Once established, it might need finance to open a second location or buy a new delivery van.

5.2
Sources of Finance
Internal and external sources of money for businesses

Internal Sources of Finance

Internal finance comes from within the business.

Retained Profit

Profit made by the business that is kept in the business instead of paid to owners as dividends. This is the cheapest source as no interest is paid and no ownership is lost.

Sale of Assets

Selling old equipment, vehicles, or property that the business no longer needs. This raises cash but may reduce the business's ability to operate efficiently.

Owner Savings

The owner putting their personal savings into the business. Common in sole traders and small partnerships.

External Sources of Finance

External finance comes from outside the business.

Bank Loans

Money borrowed from a bank that must be repaid with interest over a set period. Common for long-term needs like buying property or equipment. Banks usually want security (collateral).

Overdrafts

Allows a business to spend more from its bank account than it has. Used for short-term cash flow problems. Expensive due to high interest rates.

Share Capital

Money from investors who buy shares in the business and own a part of it. The business never has to repay this money. Common for larger companies.

Venture Capital

Money from specialist investors who invest in risky businesses (often startups) in exchange for a share of the business. They provide more than just money; they often give business advice and support.

Crowdfunding

Raising small amounts of money from many people, usually online. Reward-based crowdfunding gives backers a product in return. Equity crowdfunding means investors get shares in the business.

Grants

Money given by governments or charities that does not need to be repaid. Often available for new businesses, green businesses, or businesses in deprived areas.

Trade Credit

When suppliers allow the business to buy goods now and pay later (typically 30 days). This improves cash flow without borrowing.

Leasing

Renting equipment or vehicles rather than buying them. Keeps cash in the business but equipment is never owned.

EXAM TIP

When asked which source to recommend, consider: Is it a new business or existing? How much money is needed? How quickly is it needed? Will the business make profit to repay loans?

REAL WORLD EXAMPLE

A tech startup uses venture capital because banks won't lend without guaranteed revenue. A successful retailer might use retained profit to expand because they have cash. A small business uses trade credit to manage cash flow while waiting for customer payments.

5.3
Cash Flow
Understanding money movement and cash flow forecasting

What Is Cash Flow?

Cash flow is the movement of money in and out of a business. It shows the timing of cash, not whether the business is profitable. A business can be profitable but still run out of cash if money comes in after it needs to go out.

KEY TERM
Cash Flow

The movement of money into and out of a business over a period of time.

Cash Inflows and Outflows

Cash inflows are money coming into the business. Cash outflows are money going out.

Common Cash Inflows
  • Sales revenue (cash from customers)
  • Loans from banks
  • Owner investment or capital
  • Sale of assets
  • Money from investors
Common Cash Outflows
  • Wages and salaries
  • Rent and rates
  • Utilities (gas, electricity, water)
  • Supplier payments
  • Equipment and machinery purchases
  • Loan repayments
  • Marketing expenses
  • Taxation

Net Cash Flow

Net cash flow is the difference between cash inflows and outflows in a period.

NET CASH FLOW
Cash Inflows - Cash Outflows

Opening and Closing Balances

Opening balance is the cash at the start of the period. Closing balance is the cash at the end. The closing balance of one period becomes the opening balance of the next period.

CLOSING BALANCE
Opening Balance + Net Cash Flow
Cash Flow Example

January: Opening cash $5,000

Inflows: Sales $8,000, Loan $3,000 = $11,000

Outflows: Wages $4,000, Rent $2,000, Supplies $1,500 = $7,500

Net cash flow = $11,000 - $7,500 = $3,500

Closing balance = $5,000 + $3,500 = $8,500

Cash Flow Forecasts

A cash flow forecast predicts future cash movements. It helps businesses plan and spot potential cash shortages before they happen. Most useful for new businesses and seasonal businesses.

Cash Flow Problems and Solutions

Cash flow problems occur when outflows exceed inflows for several months, even if the business is profitable.

Common Causes
  • Seasonal demand (low sales in some months)
  • Credit terms (customers pay months after purchase)
  • Unexpected costs
  • Poor management of stock and inventory
  • Slow payment from customers
Solutions to Cash Flow Problems
  • Offer discounts for early payment from customers
  • Negotiate longer payment terms with suppliers
  • Arrange an overdraft facility with the bank
  • Reduce stock levels to free up cash
  • Delay non-urgent purchases
  • Lease equipment instead of buying
  • Sell unused assets for cash
EXAM TIP

Remember: profit and cash flow are different. A business can be profitable but run out of cash. A business can have negative cash flow in one month but still be profitable overall.

REAL WORLD EXAMPLE

A toy shop buys stock in August for the Christmas rush. They pay suppliers upfront but don't receive customer cash until October-December. This timing gap creates severe cash flow problems before Christmas even though the business is very profitable.

5.4
Income Statements
Understanding profit and loss statements

What Is an Income Statement?

An income statement (also called a profit and loss account or P and L) shows whether a business made a profit or loss during a period (usually one year). It lists all revenues and costs to show the final profit or loss.

Structure of an Income Statement

An income statement has a standard format:

REVENUE (or Sales)
Price per unit x Number of units sold
Example

If a shop sells 1000 items at $50 each: Revenue = 1000 x 50 = $50,000

TOTAL COSTS
Cost of Goods Sold + Operating Expenses
Example

COGS $255,000 + Operating Expenses $134,000 = Total Costs $389,000

GROSS PROFIT
Revenue - Cost of Goods Sold
Example

If revenue is $500,000 and COGS is $255,000: Gross Profit = $500,000 - $255,000 = $245,000

PROFIT
Revenue - Total Costs
Example

If revenue is $500,000 and total costs are $389,000: Net Profit = $500,000 - $389,000 = $111,000

Key Sections Explained

Revenue (Sales)

Total money from selling goods or services before any costs are deducted. Also called turnover.

Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)

The cost of materials and production directly related to making the goods sold. Includes raw materials and direct labour costs.

Gross Profit

Profit after deducting the direct costs of making goods but before deducting operating expenses. A high gross profit is good.

Operating Expenses

Costs needed to run the business that are not directly related to making the product. Includes rent, wages for administrative staff, utilities, marketing, and insurance.

Net Profit

The final profit after all costs including operating expenses have been deducted. This is what the business has left to pay tax or distribute to owners.

Profit Margins

Profit margins show what percentage of revenue becomes profit. They help compare profitability between different businesses.

GROSS PROFIT MARGIN %
(Gross Profit / Revenue) x 100
Example

If gross profit is $245,000 and revenue is $500,000: GPM = (245,000 / 500,000) x 100 = 49%

This means 49 cents of every dollar of sales is gross profit.

NET PROFIT MARGIN %
(Net Profit / Revenue) x 100
Example

If net profit is $111,000 and revenue is $500,000: NPM = (111,000 / 500,000) x 100 = 22.2%

This means 22.2 cents of every dollar of sales becomes net profit.

EXAM TIP

Know the order of sections in an income statement. Revenue comes first, then COGS, then operating expenses. Practice calculating all profit figures.

REAL WORLD EXAMPLE

A supermarket might have 25% gross profit margin (typical for food retail) but only 2-3% net profit margin because of high operating costs like labour, rent, and energy. A luxury brand might have 50-60% gross profit margin and 15-20% net profit margin.

5.5
Statement of Financial Position
Understanding balance sheets and what they show

What Is a Statement of Financial Position?

A statement of financial position (also called a balance sheet) shows what a business owns and what it owes at a specific moment in time. It presents a snapshot of the business's financial position. Unlike the income statement which shows profit over time, the balance sheet shows assets, liabilities, and equity at one point (usually the year end).

The Balance Sheet Equation

The balance sheet is based on a simple equation:

BALANCE SHEET EQUATION
Assets = Liabilities + Equity

What Are Assets?

Assets are things the business owns that have value. They are divided into two types:

Non-Current (Fixed) Assets

Assets that will be kept and used for more than one year:

  • Land and property
  • Machinery and equipment
  • Vehicles
  • Furniture and fixtures
Current Assets

Assets that will be converted to cash within one year:

  • Cash in the bank
  • Money owed by customers (receivables)
  • Stock and inventory
  • Short term investments

What Are Liabilities?

Liabilities are debts or amounts owed by the business. They are divided into two types:

Non-Current (Long Term) Liabilities

Debts that must be paid back after more than one year:

  • Long term bank loans
  • Mortgages
  • Bonds or debentures
Current Liabilities

Debts that must be paid within one year:

  • Money owed to suppliers (payables)
  • Short term bank loans and overdrafts
  • Taxes owed
  • Wages owed to employees

What Is Equity?

Equity is the owner's stake in the business. It is what's left if all liabilities are paid off. Equity consists of:

  • Share capital (money invested by owners/shareholders)
  • Retained profit (profit kept in the business instead of paid as dividends)

Understanding the Balance Sheet

The balance sheet equation must always balance: Total Assets = Total Liabilities + Equity.

What the Balance Sheet Tells Us

By analyzing the balance sheet, we can learn:

  • Solvency: Can the business pay its debts?
  • Liquidity: Does the business have enough current assets to cover current liabilities?
  • Ownership: How much of the business belongs to owners vs creditors?
  • Asset structure: What type of assets does the business hold?
EXAM TIP

Remember the balance sheet equation: Assets = Liabilities + Equity. Always check that this balances. Know the difference between current and non-current: current is within one year; non-current is more than one year.

REAL WORLD EXAMPLE

Apple's balance sheet shows massive cash holdings (current asset), property and factories (non-current asset), and relatively low liabilities because it is very profitable. A startup's balance sheet might show mostly non-current assets (equipment, premises) with minimal cash but high liabilities (loans).

5.6
Financial Analysis
Using ratios to assess business financial health

Why Analyze Financial Statements?

Looking at raw numbers like "profit is 100,000 pounds" doesn't tell the whole story. Is that good or bad? Compared to what? Financial ratios allow us to compare businesses of different sizes, track performance over time, and identify strengths and weaknesses.

Profitability Ratios

These ratios show how efficiently a business turns revenue into profit.

Gross Profit Margin
GROSS PROFIT MARGIN %
(Gross Profit / Revenue) x 100

What it shows: What percentage of sales revenue is available as gross profit after paying for goods. A higher percentage is better.

Example

Gross profit $100,000 / Revenue $400,000 x 100 = 25%

This means 25 cents of every dollar of sales is gross profit.

Net Profit Margin
NET PROFIT MARGIN %
(Net Profit / Revenue) x 100

What it shows: What percentage of sales revenue becomes net profit after all costs. Shows overall profitability.

Example

Net profit $50,000 / Revenue $400,000 x 100 = 12.5%

This means 12.5 cents of every dollar of sales becomes net profit.

Liquidity Ratios

These ratios show whether a business has enough liquid assets (cash) to pay its short-term debts.

Current Ratio
CURRENT RATIO
Current Assets / Current Liabilities

What it shows: How many times a business can cover its current liabilities with current assets. A ratio of 1.5 or higher is usually healthy. Below 1.0 means the business cannot cover short-term debts.

Example

Current assets $60,000 / Current liabilities $40,000 = 1.5

The business can cover its debts 1.5 times with current assets. This is healthy.

Acid Test Ratio (Quick Ratio)
ACID TEST RATIO
(Current Assets - Stock) / Current Liabilities

What it shows: Like the current ratio but excludes stock (the hardest to convert to cash quickly). A ratio of 1.0 or higher is good. This is more conservative than the current ratio.

Example

Current assets $60,000 - Stock $20,000 = $40,000 / Current liabilities $40,000 = 1.0

The business can just cover its debts without selling any stock. This is acceptable.

Interpreting Ratios

Ratio analysis is most useful when comparing:

  • A business's ratios over several years (trend analysis)
  • Different businesses in the same industry
  • A business against industry averages

A ratio by itself doesn't tell you much. Context matters:

  • Different industries have different typical ratios
  • Seasonal businesses have variation through the year
  • A declining ratio is worse than a low ratio if it was previously higher
  • Growing businesses may have lower current ratios due to investment
EXAM TIP

When asked to interpret ratios, always say what they show, calculate correctly, and explain what the numbers mean for the business. Always compare to something (previous year, competitor, or industry average) to make a judgment.

REAL WORLD EXAMPLE

A grocery chain might have 2-3% net profit margin (typical for the industry due to high costs and strong competition) while a software company might have 15-20% (lower costs, higher margins). A retailer during a growth phase might have a low current ratio of 0.8 because it's investing heavily, but this doesn't mean it's in trouble. The same ratio would be concerning for a stable, mature business.
