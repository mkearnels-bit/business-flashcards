2.1
Organisation Structures
Why businesses need structures and how they differ

What is Organisational Structure?

An organisational structure shows how a business is arranged. It explains who reports to whom, how decisions are made, and how information flows through the business. Structures help businesses operate efficiently and make sure everyone knows their role.

KEY TERM
Organisational Structure

The way a business arranges its staff and departments, showing the chain of command and how power is distributed.

Hierarchy and Chain of Command

Most businesses have a hierarchy, which means there are different levels of authority. The chain of command is the line of authority from the top of the business down to the bottom. It shows who each person reports to.

For example, a worker might report to a supervisor, who reports to a manager, who reports to a director. This clear chain helps with communication and decision making.

Span of Control

The span of control is the number of employees that one manager is responsible for. A wide span means one manager oversees many employees. A narrow span means one manager oversees only a few employees.

Wide Span of Control Narrow Span of Control
One manager supervises many employees One manager supervises few employees
Fewer management levels needed More management levels needed
Faster decision making More detailed supervision
Less detailed control Better quality control

Tall vs Flat Structures

A tall structure has many levels of management. There is a long chain of command from top to bottom. A flat structure has few levels. There is a short chain of command.

Tall Structure Advantages
  • Clear chain of command and roles
  • Narrow span of control means detailed management
  • Many opportunities for promotion
  • Better quality control
Tall Structure Disadvantages
  • Slow communication up and down
  • Expensive with many managers needed
  • Workers may feel distant from senior management
  • Slow decision making
Flat Structure Advantages
  • Fast communication and decision making
  • Cheaper with fewer managers
  • Workers feel more involved and motivated
  • Wide span of control gives autonomy
Flat Structure Disadvantages
  • Managers may be overloaded
  • Few promotion opportunities
  • Difficult to manage quality
  • Unclear roles and responsibilities

Centralised vs Decentralised

In a centralised structure, decisions are made at the top by senior management. Workers have little say in decisions. In a decentralised structure, decisions are made at different levels throughout the business. Workers have more power to make decisions.

Delegation

Delegation means giving employees responsibility and authority to make decisions or complete tasks. Managers delegate tasks to reduce their workload and develop staff skills. It helps employees feel more motivated and trusted.

REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE
Tesco and Delegation

Tesco uses delegation extensively. Store managers delegate authority to department heads, who then delegate to team leaders. This speeds up decision making in stores and helps staff develop new skills.

Different Job Roles

  • Directors: Top level. Make major strategic decisions for the whole business.
  • Managers: Middle level. Implement directors' decisions and manage departments or teams.
  • Supervisors: Lower middle level. Oversee workers and ensure quality and productivity.
  • Operatives: Bottom level. Carry out daily tasks and production work.
EXAM TIP

When asked about structures, always explain chain of command and span of control. Use a diagram if possible. Questions often ask why a business might change from tall to flat, so know the advantages and disadvantages of each.

Key Takeaways

  • Organisational structure shows how a business is arranged and who reports to whom
  • Chain of command is the line of authority from top to bottom
  • Span of control affects how many staff one manager supervises
  • Tall structures have many levels; flat structures have few levels
  • Tall structures provide detail but are slow and expensive; flat structures are fast and cheap but harder to manage
  • Delegation helps develop staff and reduce management burden

Recruitment and Selection
Finding and choosing the right employees

Why Businesses Recruit

Businesses need to recruit new employees for several reasons: to replace workers who leave, to support business growth, to fill new positions, or to bring in specialist skills the business needs.

Internal vs External Recruitment

Internal Recruitment External Recruitment
What it is: Promoting existing employees from within the business What it is: Hiring people from outside the business
Advantages: Candidates know the business already, cheaper, motivates other staff, faster process Advantages: Wider choice of candidates, brings fresh ideas and perspectives, no impact on existing staff
Disadvantages: Limited choice of candidates, no fresh ideas, leaves a gap elsewhere in business Disadvantages: Expensive, longer recruitment process, new staff need training

The Recruitment Process

Recruitment Process Flowchart Interactive
Click on each step to learn more about the recruitment process

Step by Step Process

  1. Job Analysis: The business identifies what the job involves and what skills are needed.
  2. Job Description: A document listing what the job involves, responsibilities, and working conditions.
  3. Person Specification: A document listing the qualifications, skills, experience, and personal qualities needed.
  4. Advertising: The job is advertised through job boards, websites, or newspapers to attract candidates.
  5. Shortlisting: The business reviews applications and selects the best candidates to interview.
  6. Selection Methods: Interviews, tests, or assessment centres are used to choose the best candidate.
  7. Appointment: The successful candidate is offered the job.

Selection Methods

Interviews: Face to face or video meetings where candidates answer questions about their experience and suitability. Good for assessing communication skills and personality fit.

Tests: Written tests, skills tests, or personality tests to assess candidate abilities. Useful for technical roles.

Assessment Centres: Events where multiple candidates complete various activities and tests. Good for assessing how people work in teams.

Types of Employment Contract

Full time: Working a standard number of hours per week, usually 35 to 40 hours. Permanent or temporary.

Part time: Working fewer hours per week than full time. Gives flexibility to workers.

Job share: Two people share one full time job, each working part of the week.

Zero hour contract: Worker is available to work whenever the business needs them, but no guaranteed hours. Provides maximum flexibility for business.

REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE
McDonald's and Zero Hour Contracts

McDonald's uses zero hour contracts for many of its workers. This allows it to adjust staff numbers based on customer demand, such as having more workers during busy lunch times.

EXAM TIP

Questions often compare internal and external recruitment. Remember: internal is cheaper and faster but has limited choice. External is more expensive and slower but brings fresh ideas. Also be ready to explain the difference between job description and person specification.

Key Takeaways

  • Businesses recruit to replace leavers, support growth, or fill new positions
  • Internal recruitment is cheaper and faster; external recruitment gives wider choice
  • Job description lists job duties; person specification lists required qualifications and skills
  • Selection methods include interviews, tests, and assessment centres
  • Employment types range from full time permanent to zero hour contracts

Training and Development
Developing employee skills and knowledge

Why Training is Important

Training develops employee skills and knowledge. It increases productivity, improves quality of work, keeps staff motivated, helps staff adapt to change, and improves staff retention. Well trained employees make fewer mistakes and provide better customer service.

KEY TERM
Training

A process of teaching employees the skills and knowledge needed to do their job effectively.

Induction Training

Induction training is given to all new employees. It covers the business, its policies, health and safety rules, IT systems, and introduces the new employee to their team. Good induction training helps new staff settle in quickly and understand the business culture.

On the Job Training

On the job training happens while the employee works. Methods include coaching, mentoring, shadowing, and job rotation.

On the Job Training Advantages
  • Practical and relevant to actual job
  • No time away from work
  • Cost effective
  • Immediate application of learning
On the Job Training Disadvantages
  • May slow down production
  • Trainer may not be skilled at teaching
  • Limited range of learning
  • Distractions on the work floor

Off the Job Training

Off the job training happens away from the workplace. Methods include courses, workshops, and online learning. Staff attend colleges, training providers, or company training centres.

Off the Job Training Advantages
  • Structured and comprehensive
  • Expert trainers deliver content
  • Fewer work distractions
  • Broader range of topics
Off the Job Training Disadvantages
  • Expensive
  • Time away from work
  • May not be directly relevant to role
  • Takes time to apply learning

Costs and Benefits

For the Business: Benefits include higher productivity, better quality work, improved customer service, and lower staff turnover. Costs include trainer time, course fees, and potential loss of productivity while training.

For the Employee: Benefits include developing new skills, improved career prospects, and increased confidence. Personal development also increases motivation and job satisfaction.

REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE
Google and Employee Development

Google invests heavily in employee training and development. They offer online courses, workshops, and 20% time for employees to work on personal projects. This has led to high employee retention and a reputation as a great place to work.

EXAM TIP

When comparing on the job and off the job training, remember: on the job is practical and cheap but may be disruptive; off the job is thorough and high quality but expensive. Questions often ask which is better for specific situations.

Key Takeaways

  • Training increases productivity, quality, and motivation
  • Induction training introduces new employees to the business
  • On the job training includes coaching, mentoring, shadowing, and job rotation
  • Off the job training includes courses, workshops, and online learning
  • On the job training is practical but disruptive; off the job is thorough but expensive
2.4
Motivation
What motivates workers and how businesses motivate staff

Why Motivation Matters

Motivated employees are more productive, produce higher quality work, stay with the business longer, and have lower absenteeism. Poor motivation leads to high staff turnover, low productivity, and poor quality work. Motivating staff is essential for business success.

Taylor's Scientific Management

Frederick Taylor believed workers are motivated purely by money. He suggested breaking jobs into small, simple tasks and paying workers based on how much they produce (piece rate). Workers would be more productive if paid more for producing more.

This approach increased productivity but workers became bored with repetitive tasks and felt used. Modern businesses recognise that money is only one factor in motivation.

Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs

Abraham Maslow suggested people have different levels of needs that must be satisfied in order. Only when lower level needs are met can people focus on higher level needs. Understanding this helps businesses motivate staff.

Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs Interactive
The pyramid shows Maslow's five levels of needs, from basic needs at the bottom to self actualisation at the top

Physiological Needs (bottom): Food, water, shelter, rest. In business, this means paying enough for basic living.

Safety Needs: Security, protection, stable employment. In business, this means job security and safe working conditions.

Social Needs: Friendship, belonging, teamwork. In business, this means positive workplace relationships and team working.

Esteem Needs: Respect, recognition, achievement. In business, this means praise, recognition, and opportunities to achieve.

Self Actualisation (top): Reaching full potential, personal development. In business, this means challenging work and career development opportunities.

Herzberg's Two Factor Theory

Frederick Herzberg identified two separate factors affecting motivation. Hygiene factors prevent dissatisfaction but do not create motivation. Motivators actually create motivation and satisfaction.

Herzberg's Two Factor Theory Interactive
Hygiene factors prevent dissatisfaction; motivators create satisfaction

Hygiene Factors: Pay, working conditions, company policies, job security, relationships with managers. If these are poor, workers are dissatisfied. If improved, workers are no longer dissatisfied but still not necessarily motivated.

Motivators: Achievement, recognition, responsibility, interesting work, advancement opportunities. These create real motivation and satisfaction. Workers want challenging work that they are recognised for.

EXAM TIP

Do not mix up Herzberg's hygiene factors with motivation. A question asking how to motivate employees should focus on motivators (recognition, achievement, responsibility), not just improving hygiene factors like pay. Better pay prevents dissatisfaction but does not necessarily increase motivation.

Mayo's Hawthorne Studies

Elton Mayo studied workers and found that social needs and teamwork are important for motivation. Workers are motivated not just by money but by feeling part of a team, having good relationships with colleagues, and being noticed and valued.

Financial Motivation Methods

  • Wages and Salaries: Regular payments for work. Helps meet physiological and safety needs.
  • Bonuses: Extra payments for good performance. Encourages high quality work.
  • Commission: Payment based on sales or output. Used in sales roles.
  • Profit Sharing: Employees share in company profits. Creates sense of ownership.
  • Piece Rate: Payment per item produced. Motivates high output but may reduce quality.

Non Financial Motivation Methods

  • Job Rotation: Employees change roles regularly to avoid boredom and develop new skills.
  • Job Enlargement: Give employees more tasks at the same level to make work less repetitive.
  • Job Enrichment: Give employees more responsibility and interesting work. Herzberg's key motivator.
  • Empowerment: Give employees power to make decisions about their work without asking managers first.
  • Teamworking: Organise work in teams to improve social needs and belonging.
  • Training and Development: Help employees develop skills and advance their careers.
  • Promotion Opportunities: Clear paths for advancement motivate ambitious employees.
  • Recognition and Praise: Public recognition of good work. Low cost but very motivating.
  • Fringe Benefits: Non financial rewards like gym membership, pension, or flexible hours.
REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE
Netflix and Motivation

Netflix uses empowerment and autonomy to motivate staff. Employees have freedom to make decisions about their work and can take unlimited holiday. The company focuses on results rather than hours worked, creating a culture of trust and responsibility.

Key Takeaways

  • Motivated staff are more productive and stay with the business longer
  • Taylor believed workers are motivated only by money through piece rate pay
  • Maslow identified five levels of needs that must be satisfied in order
  • Herzberg's hygiene factors prevent dissatisfaction; motivators create satisfaction
  • Mayo found that social needs and teamwork are important for motivation
  • Financial methods include wages, bonuses, commission, and profit sharing
  • Non financial methods include job enrichment, empowerment, recognition, and development

Leadership and Management
Different leadership styles and their effects

Leadership vs Management

Management is about planning, organising, and controlling resources to achieve business goals. Leadership is about inspiring and motivating people to follow a vision. A good manager may not be a good leader, and vice versa. Effective businesses need both.

Leadership Styles

There are three main leadership styles: autocratic, democratic, and laissez faire.

Autocratic Leadership

The leader makes all decisions and tells employees what to do. Workers have no input into decisions. This style is appropriate in emergencies or for unskilled workers who need direction.

Autocratic Leadership Advantages
  • Fast decision making
  • Clear expectations and rules
  • Good for emergencies
  • Works with unskilled workers
Autocratic Leadership Disadvantages
  • Low staff motivation
  • High staff turnover
  • No new ideas from staff
  • Workers feel undervalued

Democratic Leadership

The leader involves employees in decisions. Staff are asked for their views and ideas before decisions are made. This style is appropriate for skilled workers and most modern businesses.

Democratic Leadership Advantages
  • High staff motivation
  • Better ideas from employees
  • Good staff retention
  • Better quality decisions
Democratic Leadership Disadvantages
  • Slow decision making
  • Takes time to consult
  • Conflicts may arise
  • Responsibility unclear

Laissez Faire Leadership

The leader takes a hands off approach. Employees are given complete freedom to make decisions and do their work as they see fit. The leader provides little direction or support. This style works only with highly skilled and motivated employees.

Laissez Faire Leadership Advantages
  • Maximum employee freedom
  • Staff feel trusted and valued
  • Good for creative work
  • High motivation for skilled staff
Laissez Faire Leadership Disadvantages
  • No clear direction or control
  • Can lead to chaos
  • Poor for unskilled workers
  • Lack of accountability

How Leadership Style Affects Motivation and Performance

Autocratic: Can motivate workers through clear expectations but often leads to low motivation as staff feel undervalued. Productivity may be high in short term but staff retention is poor.

Democratic: Usually leads to higher motivation as staff feel involved and valued. Better quality decisions and lower staff turnover. Slightly slower decision making.

Laissez Faire: Can lead to very high motivation for skilled, creative workers who want autonomy. However, can lead to chaos and poor performance with unskilled workers or in crisis situations.

KEY TERM
Leadership Style

The approach a leader uses to guide and influence their employees in achieving business goals.

REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE
Jeff Bezos and Amazon's Approach

Amazon uses a mix of democratic and laissez faire leadership. The company encourages employees to innovate and make decisions, but has very high performance expectations. This mix motivates talented staff but Amazon has a reputation for working employees hard.

Key Takeaways

  • Leadership inspires people; management organises resources
  • Autocratic leaders make all decisions; appropriate in emergencies
  • Democratic leaders involve staff; creates high motivation
  • Laissez faire leaders give freedom; works only with skilled staff
  • Leadership style significantly affects staff motivation and business performance
2.6
Communication
Effective communication in business

Why Effective Communication Matters

Effective communication is essential for business success. Poor communication leads to misunderstandings, mistakes, low morale, and poor customer service. Good communication helps staff understand their role, makes decisions faster, and improves customer relationships.

Internal vs External Communication

Internal communication: Communication within the business between employees, departments, and management. Examples include meetings, emails, memos, notices.

External communication: Communication between the business and people outside it, such as customers, suppliers, banks, or the government. Examples include customer service calls, supplier emails, advertising.

Formal vs Informal Communication

Formal communication: Official, structured communication following set channels and procedures. Examples include official memos, reports, meetings with agendas. Appropriate for important decisions.

Informal communication: Casual communication between people. Examples include chat between colleagues, casual phone calls, notes. Faster but may be misunderstood.

Methods of Communication

  • Verbal: Face to face meetings, phone calls, presentations. Allows instant feedback and questions. Risk of misunderstanding if no record made.
  • Written: Emails, letters, reports, memos. Creates a record. Can be reviewed. Takes longer than verbal.
  • Visual: Charts, diagrams, presentations, pictures. Helps understand complex information. Works well with data.
  • Electronic: Email, video calls, social media, messaging apps. Fast and can reach many people. Risk of information getting lost in volume.

One Way vs Two Way Communication

One way communication: Information flows in one direction only. Examples include announcements, lectures, advertising. Fast but no feedback or questions.

Two way communication: Information flows both ways; receiver can respond and ask questions. Examples include meetings, conversations, phone calls. Slower but allows discussion and clarification.

Barriers to Communication

  • Jargon: Using complicated words people do not understand. Solution: use simple, clear language.
  • Noise: Loud environment makes it hard to hear. Solution: choose quiet location for important conversations.
  • Language Barriers: Employees speak different languages. Solution: use translators or simple language.
  • Distance: Physical distance between people makes communication hard. Solution: use video calls or meetings.
  • Too Many Layers: In tall structures, messages get lost as they pass through many managers. Solution: use flat structure or direct communication.
  • Lack of Time: Busy managers do not listen properly. Solution: schedule dedicated communication time.
  • Receiver Not Listening: People not paying attention. Solution: choose appropriate time and ensure engagement.

How to Overcome Barriers

  • Use simple, clear language that everyone understands
  • Choose appropriate methods for the message
  • Choose appropriate times and quiet locations
  • Check understanding by asking questions
  • Use visual aids to explain complex ideas
  • Encourage two way communication and feedback
  • Reduce the number of communication layers in tall structures
  • Use multiple methods to reinforce important messages

Technology and Communication

Technology has changed communication dramatically. Email allows instant written communication. Video calls replace physical meetings. Social media allows real time communication with customers. However, too much technology can lead to information overload and less personal relationships.

REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE
Slack and Workplace Communication

Slack provides instant messaging for teams. It speeds up communication and reduces email overload. However, it can also lead to constant interruptions and people feeling they must respond immediately. Businesses must find the right balance.

EXAM TIP

Questions about communication often ask about barriers and how to overcome them. Always give specific examples and explain why barriers are a problem. Also be ready to discuss when to use formal vs informal, written vs verbal, or one way vs two way communication.

Key Takeaways

  • Effective communication is essential for business success
  • Internal communication is within the business; external is with outside people
  • Formal communication follows procedures; informal is casual
  • Different methods suit different situations
  • One way communication is fast but has no feedback; two way allows discussion
  • Barriers include jargon, noise, language, distance, and too many layers
  • Overcome barriers by using simple language, appropriate methods, and two way communication

Trade Unions
What trade unions do and their impact on business

What is a Trade Union?

A trade union is an organisation of workers in a particular industry or profession. Members pay fees to the union. The union represents members' interests and negotiates with employers on their behalf.

KEY TERM
Trade Union

An organisation of workers that represents members' interests and negotiates with employers about pay and working conditions.

Why Employees Join Trade Unions

  • Better pay and working conditions through collective bargaining
  • Protection against unfair treatment
  • Legal support if in disputes with employer
  • Improved job security
  • Voice in decisions affecting workers
  • Strength in numbers when negotiating

Role of Trade Unions

Collective Bargaining: The union negotiates with the employer on behalf of all members. They negotiate about pay, hours, holidays, working conditions, and other terms of employment. A single worker has little power; workers together have bargaining power.

Protecting Workers' Rights: Unions ensure employers follow employment laws and treat workers fairly. They represent members if they are dismissed unfairly or discriminated against.

Health and Safety: Unions negotiate for safe working conditions and check employers are following health and safety laws.

Training and Development: Some unions offer training to members to improve their skills and career prospects.

Industrial Action

If negotiations fail, unions may take industrial action to pressure employers to agree to their demands.

  • Strikes: Workers stop work. The most powerful form of action as it stops production. Can harm business significantly but workers lose pay.
  • Work to Rule: Workers follow every rule and procedure exactly, even if it slows down work. No lost pay but less disruptive than strikes.
  • Overtime Bans: Workers refuse to work extra hours. Reduces production but less damaging than strikes.
  • Go Slow: Workers deliberately slow down production. Subtle pressure on business.

Impact of Trade Unions on Businesses

Positive Impacts: Unions ensure fair treatment of workers, which can reduce staff turnover and improve morale. When unions and management work together, it can lead to more stable employment relationships. Good working conditions lead to higher productivity.

Negative Impacts: Unions may demand higher pay and better conditions that increase business costs. Industrial action disrupts production and loses sales. Management has less flexibility to change working practices quickly. Potential conflict between management and unions.

How Businesses and Unions Work Together

  • Regular meetings to discuss issues before they become problems
  • Clear communication about business plans and challenges
  • Genuine negotiation where both sides listen to each other
  • Joint committees to discuss health and safety, training, and working conditions
  • Agreement to arbitration if disagreements cannot be resolved
  • Both sides recognising the need to keep the business viable
REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE
The 2023 UK Rail Strikes

UK rail workers' unions called strikes over pay and conditions. This caused major disruption to millions of commuters. The strikes highlighted both the power of unions and the impact of industrial action on customers and the economy.

EXAM TIP

Questions about trade unions often ask about their impact on business. Be balanced: explain both benefits (fair treatment, stable workforce) and drawbacks (higher costs, potential conflict). Also know the different types of industrial action and when unions might use them.

Key Takeaways

  • Trade unions represent workers' interests and negotiate with employers
  • Workers join unions for better pay, conditions, and protection
  • Unions use collective bargaining to negotiate on behalf of all members
  • Industrial action includes strikes, work to rule, and overtime bans
  • Unions can improve working conditions but also increase business costs
  • Businesses and unions can work together for mutual benefit
